## Thailand (18 Events)

### Childhood

#### th_childhood_001: The Novice Ordination Festival
**Cultural Practice:** The *poy sang long* (novice ordination festival) is a major Shan cultural event in which young boys aged 8-12 are dressed as princes, carried to the monastery on relatives' shoulders, and ordained as novices. The ordination generates enormous merit for parents, especially mothers, as it is the primary way a son "repays" the debt to his mother.
**Academic Source:** Eberhardt, *Living Buddhism*, Chapter 8: "Imagined Lives," pp. 143-146. Describes boys Tsit and Weng anticipating ordination, and the cultural framework of ordination as a life-course marker. Additional context in Chapter 6 on ordination festivals as village-wide events.
**Game Event Source Field:** `Scholar 2 (Eberhardt Ch8 -- Tsit and Weng anticipating ordination)`

#### th_childhood_002: The Temple School
**Cultural Practice:** The village monastery (*wat*) serves as both a religious institution and a community center in Shan villages. Temples provide education, host village meetings, and serve as overnight accommodation for travelers. The monastery is the center of community life, containing a preaching hall, pagoda, ordination hall, and dwellings for monks, novices, and *mae khaao* (Buddhist nuns).
**Academic Source:** Eberhardt, *Living Buddhism*, Chapter 1, pp. 16-18. Describes the monastery's multifunctional role in village life.
**Game Event Source Field:** `Knowledge base: Thai Buddhist institutions -- temple as community center`
**Knowledge Base Trace:** Knowledge base entry draws from Eberhardt Ch1, pp. 16-18.

#### th_childhood_003: The Spirit at the Crossroads
**Cultural Practice:** Shan communities maintain shrines to the *tsao moeng* (village guardian spirit) at the edge of the village, near the forest. Children's *khwan* (souls) are considered more tenuously attached and thus more vulnerable to spirit interference. Spirit encounters near the shrine are taken seriously as they can cause illness.
**Academic Source:** Eberhardt, *Living Buddhism*, Chapter 2, pp. 30-31 (tsao moeng shrine and its role), pp. 41-43 (children's vulnerable khwan).
**Game Event Source Field:** `Knowledge base: Shan practice -- tsao moeng shrine, children's vulnerable khwan`
**Knowledge Base Trace:** Knowledge base entry draws from Eberhardt Ch2, pp. 30-31, 41-43.

### Adolescence

#### th_adolescence_001: The Paying Respect Ceremony
**Cultural Practice:** The *kaentau* (Paying Respect ceremony) is a biannual village-wide ritual in which young people visit *khon naun tsaung* (temple sleepers -- devout lay elders who stay at the temple regularly) with flower bouquets and gifts. It reinforces the generational hierarchy and honors those who have committed to intensive spiritual practice in old age.
**Academic Source:** Eberhardt, *Living Buddhism*, Chapter 7: "Aging and Overall Development." Describes kaentau ceremony and the social role of temple sleepers in the community.
**Game Event Source Field:** `Scholar 2 (Eberhardt Ch7 -- kaentau Paying Respect ceremony)`

#### th_adolescence_002: The Amulet Vendor
**Cultural Practice:** Protective amulets, tattoos, and verbal formulae (*katha*) are supplementary sources of personal power that coexist with the merit system. Amulets are sold near temples and are believed to offer physical protection and good fortune. Power (*amoral*) from amulets supplements merit throughout life.
**Academic Source:** Eberhardt, *Living Buddhism*, Chapter 3, pp. 50-51. Describes the relationship between merit and alternative power sources including amulets and tattoos.
**Game Event Source Field:** `Knowledge base: Thai distinctive practices -- amulets and protective objects`
**Knowledge Base Trace:** Knowledge base entry draws from Eberhardt Ch3, pp. 50-51.

### Young Adulthood

#### th_youngadult_001: Someone Asks for Your Food
**Cultural Practice:** In Shan culture, if you have something and someone asks for it, you must give it or share it. If food is being eaten, the cultural imperative is to invite anyone who makes eye contact. This creates a leveling mechanism: the more status you claim, the more people will ask things of you. The only acceptable deflection is claiming you do not have the item (*am mii*).
**Academic Source:** Eberhardt, *Living Buddhism*, Chapter 4. Details the asking-for-things dynamic as a social mechanic governing resource distribution and status.
**Game Event Source Field:** `Scholar 1 (Eberhardt Ch4 -- asking-for-things dynamic)`

#### th_youngadult_002: Gambling at the Funeral
**Cultural Practice:** Gambling beneath the house during funeral vigils is a longstanding Shan tradition. It serves a social function: keeping enough people at the vigil to provide company and security during the days between death and cremation. Entrepreneurial women set up food stalls near the gambling area.
**Academic Source:** Eberhardt, *Living Buddhism*, Chapter 3, pp. 52-53. Describes gambling at Grandfather Wang's funeral vigil and the social justification: "If we didn't have gambling, not enough people would come."
**Game Event Source Field:** `Scholar 1 (Eberhardt Ch3 -- funeral gambling traditions)`

#### th_youngadult_003: Transgender Ordination
**Cultural Practice:** Thailand's reformed monastic tradition limits full ordination (*upasamapada*) to males, effectively barring transgender persons (*katoey*) from *bhikkhu* ordination. Khun Mae Tritrinn, a transgendered monastic teacher, established the Dantham nunnery in Chiang Dao as a "third-way" alternative, facing severe community rejection before eventual acceptance after a year-long cave retreat.
**Academic Source:** Kidpromma & Taylor (2024), "Transgenderism, Othering and Third Way Buddhist Monasticism in Chiang Mai, Thailand," pp. 175-182. Documents Khun Mae Tritrinn's journey, community rejection during alms rounds, meeting with Khruba Bunchum, and eventual acceptance.
**Game Event Source Field:** `Scholar 3 (Kidpromma -- transgender monasticism and Khun Mae Tritrinn)`

### Adulthood

#### th_adulthood_001: The Sobriety Oath
**Cultural Practice:** "Drinking the water of the *tsao moeng*" is a sobriety oath sworn before the village guardian spirit with supernatural enforcement. If broken, the *tsao moeng* punishes with madness, chronic illness, or financial ruin. Uncle Pon, a derelict alcoholic in 1980, took this oath and was subsequently chosen by the *tsao moeng* as a traditional healer (*saraa*), transforming his life entirely by 1990.
**Academic Source:** Eberhardt, *Living Buddhism*, Chapter 2, pp. 30-31 (the tsao moeng oath), pp. 28-33 (Uncle Pon's transformation from alcoholic to healer). Also documented in Case Studies T1.
**Game Event Source Field:** `Scholar 1 (Eberhardt Ch2 -- Uncle Pon's transformation)`

#### th_adulthood_002: The Egg Won't Fall
**Cultural Practice:** The *haung khwan* (soul-calling) ceremony uses a horseshoe-shaped crossbow with an egg balanced on it. When the egg finally drops into the patient's hands, the *khwan* (soul) has returned. The audience speculates about what the *khwan* wants -- money, flowers, jewelry. In Nang Ing's case, the egg fell 28 times before her gold necklace enticed the *khwan* back. In Lom's case, 21 attempts were needed.
**Academic Source:** Eberhardt, *Living Buddhism*, Chapter 2, pp. 38-42. Detailed accounts of Nang Ing's and Lom's healing sessions. Also documented in Case Studies T2 and T3.
**Game Event Source Field:** `Scholar 1 (Eberhardt Ch2 -- Nang Ing and Lom's healing sessions)`

#### th_adulthood_003: Sponsoring an Ordination
**Cultural Practice:** Sponsoring a child's ordination festival is a key marker of mature adulthood in Shan culture. It demonstrates competence, social maturity, ambition, and willingness to shoulder community responsibility. The festivals are large-scale village-wide events involving massive expenditure, community labor, feasting, and display. The lavishness of the ceremony reflects social standing.
**Academic Source:** Eberhardt, *Living Buddhism*, Chapter 6, pp. 144-146. Describes ordination as a life-course marker, the gender tensions involved (women cannot ordain but play central roles as sponsors), and wealth display dynamics.
**Game Event Source Field:** `Scholar 1 (Eberhardt Ch6 -- ordination festivals)`

#### th_adulthood_004: A Violent Death in the Village
**Cultural Practice:** When death is sudden, violent, or involves a young person, it constitutes a "dangerous death" that creates *wot* (negative karmic residue) requiring extra merit-making. The community fears that *khwan* of the violently deceased may linger, endangering the living. In the case of Nang Yen and her daughter Tsip, killed by lightning, the village demanded immediate cremation and the surviving husband Pi Non became "somewhat crazy" (*yaung*).
**Academic Source:** Eberhardt, *Living Buddhism*, Chapter 3, pp. 60-68. Documents the case of Nang Yen and Tsip's dangerous death, the community's fearful response, and the contrast with Grandfather Wang's "good death." Also documented in Case Study 5.
**Game Event Source Field:** `Scholar 1 (Eberhardt Ch3 -- Nang Yen and Tsip's dangerous death)`

#### th_adulthood_005: The Healer's Calling
**Cultural Practice:** A *saraa* (traditional healer) is chosen by the *tsao moeng* -- "one does not decide to become a healer." After Uncle Pon took the sobriety oath, the *tsao moeng* gave him healing verses (*katha*) in a dream, beginning his practice. His healing sessions combine Buddhist scripture recitation, spirit exorcism (*pat phi*), and soul-calling (*haung khwan*).
**Academic Source:** Eberhardt, *Living Buddhism*, Chapter 2, pp. 30-33. Describes how the tsao moeng chose Uncle Pon and gave him katha, and the structure of his healing practice.
**Game Event Source Field:** `Scholar 1 (Eberhardt Ch2 -- Uncle Pon's calling)`

#### th_adulthood_006: The Forest Ordination
**Cultural Practice:** Forest ordination (tying monastic robes to trees) originated in northern Thailand in the late 1980s. By wrapping a tree in a monastic robe, it becomes a sacred Buddhist monk -- "not to be killed or destroyed." The practice extends the First Precept to the natural world, drawing on Buddhadasa Bhikkhu's teaching: "Preserving ecology means conserving dhamma as dhamma is our inner ecology."
**Academic Source:** Kittisenee (2024), pp. 520-521. Documents forest ordination practices and Buddhadasa Bhikkhu's ecological Buddhism.
**Game Event Source Field:** `Knowledge base: Thai contemporary issues -- forest ordination as environmental practice`
**Knowledge Base Trace:** Knowledge base entry draws from Kittisenee (2024), pp. 520-521.

#### th_adulthood_007: Songkran Water War
**Cultural Practice:** Songkran is Thailand's traditional New Year water festival (April 13-15), in which water is thrown in blessing and celebration. It has evolved from a religious purification ritual into a massive public celebration with water fights.

### Elderhood

#### th_elderhood_001: The Aging Decision
**Cultural Practice:** The Shan life course unfolds in phases (not tied to specific ages). The transition to *khon naun tsaung* (temple sleeper) is a gradual process triggered by physical decline, children's maturity, and social expectation. Temple sleepers take eight precepts, meditate with *mak nap* (rosary beads) repeating "anicca, dukkha, anatta," and progressively withdraw from worldly life.
**Academic Source:** Eberhardt, *Living Buddhism*, Chapter 7: "Aging and Overall Development." Describes the aging transition, Uncle Toen the Great Hunter's experience with omens, and the developmental framework of Shan elderhood.
**Game Event Source Field:** `Scholar 2 (Eberhardt Ch7 -- aging and temple sleeping transitions)`

#### th_elderhood_002: The Good Death
**Cultural Practice:** A "good death" in Shan Buddhism is one that is old, meritorious, and calm -- the dying person has accumulated merit, eliminated *wot* (negative karmic residue), and approaches death with clear, unattached consciousness. Grandfather Wang's death exemplified this ideal: a 70-year-old temple sleeper whose funeral was elaborate but calm.
**Academic Source:** Eberhardt, *Living Buddhism*, Chapter 3, pp. 47-59. Detailed narrative of Grandfather Wang's "good death" and funeral, including the four-day mourning period, monk chanting, yaat nam merit-transfer, and cremation. Also documented in Case Study 4.
**Game Event Source Field:** `Scholar 1 (Eberhardt Ch3 -- Grandfather Wang's good death)`

#### th_elderhood_003: The Hunter's Omens
**Cultural Practice:** When an aging hunter/fisher begins experiencing strange omens -- missed shots at close range, empty fishing nets, a fish that traps a hand -- Shan elders interpret these as signs that the person should give up hunting and transition to temple sleeping. Uncle Toen, "the Great Hunter," experienced exactly such omens and eventually heeded them.
**Academic Source:** Eberhardt, *Living Buddhism*, Chapter 7. Documents Uncle Toen's story and the omen-based transition from worldly life to temple sleeping as a recognized stage in the Shan life course.
**Game Event Source Field:** `Scholar 2 (Eberhardt Ch7 -- Uncle Toen the Great Hunter)`

## Myanmar (15 Events)

### Childhood

#### mm_childhood_001: The Monastery School
**Cultural Practice:** In Myanmar, the monastery is the "sonic tapestry of everyday life" -- monks' sermons broadcast from loudspeakers are the first sound of morning. Monasteries historically provided free basic education (reading, writing, arithmetic, and Buddhist scripture) to communities where private schooling is unaffordable. Burmese vocabulary draws heavily on Pali, embedding Buddhist concepts into the language itself.
**Academic Source:** Chambers (2024), pp. 50-53, documents Buddhism as the sonic and linguistic fabric of daily life in Karen State. Edwards (2021) discusses the deep integration of Pali into Burmese language.
**Game Event Source Field:** `Knowledge base: Myanmar Buddhist institutions -- monastery as sonic tapestry`
**Knowledge Base Trace:** Knowledge base entry draws from Chambers (2024) and Edwards (2021).

#### mm_childhood_002: The Nat Shrine Offering
**Cultural Practice:** In Myanmar, *nats* (local spirits) are honored alongside Theravada Buddhism. Nat shrines coexist with Buddhist pagodas, and spirit propitiation is folded into Buddhist observance rather than being seen as contradictory. This syncretism parallels the Shan *phi* beliefs and Thai spirit practices.
**Academic Source:** Eberhardt, *Living Buddhism*, Chapter 1, pp. 10, 14 (syncretism of Buddhism and spirit beliefs in mainland Southeast Asia). Chambers (2024), pp. 53-54 (nats in Karen Buddhist practice).
**Game Event Source Field:** `Knowledge base: Myanmar distinctive practices -- nats folded into Theravada practice`
**Knowledge Base Trace:** Knowledge base entry draws from Eberhardt Ch1, pp. 10, 14 and Chambers (2024).

### Adolescence

#### mm_adolescence_001: The Shinbyu Ceremony
**Cultural Practice:** *Shinbyu* is the Burmese novice ordination ceremony, a major life event for boys that generates enormous merit for their parents. As in Shan culture, ordination is the primary way a son "repays" the debt to his mother. The ceremony demonstrates the family's spiritual commitment and social standing. The merit-power nexus means merit-making and wealth/social status are deeply intertwined.
**Academic Source:** Eberhardt, *Living Buddhism*, Chapter 6, pp. 144-146 (ordination as life-course marker and merit generation). Chambers (2024), pp. 54-55 (dana and ordination in Karen Buddhist communities, where sponsoring novice ordinations is central to demonstrating moral character).
**Game Event Source Field:** `Knowledge base: Myanmar ordination practices and merit-power nexus`
**Knowledge Base Trace:** Knowledge base entry draws from Eberhardt Ch6 and Chambers (2024).

#### mm_adolescence_002: The Metta Recitation Competition
**Cultural Practice:** The morning prayer (*awgatha*) and daily *metta* (loving-kindness) recitations are universal devotional practices in Myanmar. Metta practice begins and ends with spreading loving-kindness to all sentient beings. Recitations demonstrate devotion to the Buddha and the dhamma.
**Academic Source:** Chambers (2024), pp. 50-53 (awgatha and daily metta recitations as standard devotional practice in Karen State).
**Game Event Source Field:** `Knowledge base: Myanmar distinctive practices -- morning metta recitations`
**Knowledge Base Trace:** Knowledge base entry draws from Chambers (2024).

### Young Adulthood

#### mm_youngadult_001: The Drowning Man Pamphlet
**Cultural Practice:** Christian evangelism in Myanmar involves the distribution of tracts like "The Drowning Man" (*ye niq thu*), which depicts a person drowning as a metaphor for spiritual peril. Buddhist passengers receiving such tracts on boats consider them terrible omens (*ni mak ma kaun bu*). The encounter illustrates the complex Buddhist-Christian translation dynamics in Myanmar, where evangelists oscillate between "domesticating" (using Buddhist vocabulary) and "foreignizing" their message.
**Academic Source:** Edwards (2021), "Drowning in Context: Translating Salvation in Myanmar," *Comparative Studies of South Asia, Africa and the Middle East* 41.2. Documents the tract and its reception among Buddhist boat passengers on the Irrawaddy.
**Game Event Source Field:** `Scholar 2 (Edwards -- The Drowning Man tract on the Irrawaddy)`

#### mm_youngadult_002: Dirty Money and Right Livelihood
**Cultural Practice:** The Plong Karen concept of *thout kyar* ("keeping faithful") encompasses faithfulness to self, others, community, and land. Money from "unclean" sources (*ma than shin te paik san*) -- gambling, drugs, debt collection -- is considered morally polluting and dangerous. A villager who sold drugs was believed to have been possessed and hanged himself, illustrating the karmic dangers of dirty money.
**Academic Source:** Chambers (2024), "Brokering Morality," pp. 64-67. Documents the moral economy of unclean money, Hpi Ha Mya's warnings, and the death attributed to dirty money.
**Game Event Source Field:** `Scholar 3 (Chambers -- unclean money and the strongman economy in Karen State)`

#### mm_youngadult_003: The Saffron Revolution
**Cultural Practice:** Myanmar monks have a history of political engagement, from the 2007 Saffron Revolution to ongoing resistance against military rule. The military government sought legitimacy through strategic promotion of Buddhism. Monks' political action draws on the Buddhist moral framework to challenge state power.
**Academic Source:** Rush (2018), *SE Asia: A Very Short Introduction*, p. 7 (Myanmar's political instability). Chambers (2024), pp. 52-54 (the military-Buddhist relationship and Buddhist resistance). Knowledge base compiles from both sources.
**Game Event Source Field:** `Knowledge base: Myanmar political context -- military rule and Buddhist resistance`
**Knowledge Base Trace:** Knowledge base entry draws from Rush (2018) and Chambers (2024).

### Adulthood

#### mm_adulthood_001: The Strongman's Donation
**Cultural Practice:** Saw Eh Klein is simultaneously one of the most generous Buddhist benefactors (*saboung kachar*) in his community and a former DKBA officer whose wealth derives from controlling gambling, drugs, and predatory lending. He handles 20 million kyat per week while average household income is 100,000-150,000 kyat per month. His family's everyday acts of *dana* are genuinely praised even as his wealth sources are critiqued.
**Academic Source:** Chambers (2024), "Brokering Morality," pp. 62-67. Case Study 3 in scholar-3-contemporary.md. Documents the specific moral tensions around Saw Eh Klein's dual role.
**Game Event Source Field:** `Scholar 3 (Chambers -- Saw Eh Klein the Buddhist strongman)`

#### mm_adulthood_002: The Lottery Sermon
**Cultural Practice:** U Thuzana, a powerful charismatic Karen Buddhist monk and patron of the DKBA, encoded lottery numbers in Buddhist terminology and numerology during sermons. He joked: "It is good that so many Plong people gamble on the two-digit lottery so that I can continue to keep building as many pagodas as possible." Only those with appropriate karma could decipher the winning numbers.
**Academic Source:** Chambers (2024), "Brokering Morality," pp. 53-55. Case Study 4 in scholar-3-contemporary.md. Documents U Thuzana's lottery sermons and the interweaving of Buddhist devotion with gambling.
**Game Event Source Field:** `Scholar 3 (Chambers -- U Thuzana's lottery sermons)`

#### mm_adulthood_003: The Buddhist Word Police
**Cultural Practice:** The Sangha Maha Nayaka Committee (Myanmar's state-appointed council of senior Buddhist monks) issued statements banning non-Buddhists from using approximately 100 Pali-derived words in publications, including *metta* (loving-kindness), *dukkha* (suffering), and *paya* (a word used by Christians for God and by Buddhists for the Buddha). This reflects the state regulation of religious language.
**Academic Source:** Edwards (2021), "Drowning in Context," discusses the state regulation of religious terminology in Myanmar, the ban on Pali-derived words for Christian publications, and the anxiety around the dual use of *paya*.
**Game Event Source Field:** `Scholar 2 (Edwards -- state regulation of religious language in Myanmar)`

#### mm_adulthood_004: The Evangelist at Your Door
**Cultural Practice:** Christian door-to-door evangelism in peri-urban Yangon involves young evangelists using Buddhist vocabulary (*dukkha*, *kutho*, *che zu daw*) to communicate Christian concepts. The encounter with Daw Than Than illustrates how cross-religious encounters are fundamentally about translation -- finding "points of contact" while maintaining meaningful difference.
**Academic Source:** Edwards (2021), "Drowning in Context." Documents Daw Than Than's encounter with evangelists and the complex translation dynamics between Buddhist and Christian vocabularies.
**Game Event Source Field:** `Scholar 2 (Edwards -- Daw Than Than's encounter with evangelists)`

#### mm_adulthood_005: The Pagoda on Christian Land
**Cultural Practice:** U Thuzana, the charismatic Karen Buddhist monk, built pagodas in Christian and Muslim compounds as part of Buddhist revivalism (*thathana pyu*), asserting Buddhist territorial and spiritual dominance in ethnically diverse Karen State.
**Academic Source:** Chambers (2024), "Brokering Morality," pp. 52-54. Documents U Thuzana's pagoda-building campaigns and their role in Buddhist ethno-nationalism.
**Game Event Source Field:** `Knowledge base: Myanmar -- U Thuzana building pagodas in Christian and Muslim compounds`
**Knowledge Base Trace:** Knowledge base entry draws from Chambers (2024), pp. 52-54.

#### mm_adulthood_006: The Coup
**Cultural Practice:** The Myanmar military seized power in a coup on February 1, 2021, disrupting the democratic process. The military government sought legitimacy through strategic promotion of Buddhism. Buddhist monks and laypeople faced the dilemma of how to respond to political violence through a Buddhist moral framework.
**Academic Source:** Rush (2018), p. 7 (Myanmar's history of military rule). Chambers (2024), pp. 52-54 (military-Buddhist relationship). Knowledge base entry references the February 2021 coup specifically.
**Game Event Source Field:** `Knowledge base: Myanmar -- February 2021 military coup`
**Knowledge Base Trace:** Knowledge base entry draws from Rush (2018) and current events reporting; the political context is analyzed through the lens of Chambers (2024).

### Elderhood

#### mm_elderhood_001: The Meditation Master's Retreat
**Cultural Practice:** Advanced meditation practice in Theravada Buddhism involves cultivating *samadhi* (concentration) and experiencing *nimitta* (insight visions). Temple sleepers and elder practitioners meditate using *mak nap* (Buddhist rosary beads), repeating "anicca, dukkha, anatta." Advanced practitioners may develop spiritual powers.
**Academic Source:** Eberhardt, *Living Buddhism*, Chapter 7 (temple sleeping and meditation practice). Kidpromma & Taylor (2024), pp. 178-180 (nimitta visions experienced by Khun Mae Tritrinn).
**Game Event Source Field:** `Knowledge base: Myanmar Buddhist concepts -- nimitta, samadhi, meditation practice`
**Knowledge Base Trace:** Knowledge base entry draws from Eberhardt Ch7 and Kidpromma & Taylor (2024).

#### mm_elderhood_002: The Buddhist Nationalist Rally
**Cultural Practice:** Buddhist nationalism in Myanmar rendered Muslims especially precarious, culminating in the Rohingya crisis and ethnic cleansing. Organizations like Ma Ba Tha promoted Buddhist ethno-nationalism. The tension between Buddhism's universalist teachings of compassion and its instrumentalization for nationalist violence represents a profound moral dilemma.
**Academic Source:** Rush (2018), p. 7 (Myanmar's internal divisions and ethnic cleansing). Chambers (2024), pp. 52-54 (U Thuzana's ethno-nationalist sermons guarding against "Islamisation"). Edwards (2021) (Buddhist identity boundaries in Myanmar).

**Knowledge Base Trace:** Knowledge base entry draws from Rush (2018), Chambers (2024), and Edwards (2021).

## Cambodia (12 Events)

### Childhood

#### kh_childhood_001: The Neak Ta Shrine
**Cultural Practice:** *Neak Ta* are guardian spirits in Khmer tradition -- protectors of earth and water, with ruling power over all living beings. Their shrines are found even within Buddhist pagoda precincts, demonstrating the Cambodian syncretism of Buddhism and animism. The village exists in the *Srok* (ordered space) between the wild *Prei* (forest) and the sacred.
**Academic Source:** Kittisenee (2024), p. 514 (Neak Ta as guardian spirit with shrines in pagoda precincts). Additional context pp. 513-514 (Srok/Prei cosmological distinction).
**Game Event Source Field:** `Knowledge base: Cambodia -- Neak Ta guardian spirits with shrines in pagoda precincts`
**Knowledge Base Trace:** Knowledge base entry draws from Kittisenee (2024), pp. 513-514.

#### kh_childhood_002: Grandmother's Khmer Rouge Story
**Cultural Practice:** The Khmer Rouge regime (1975-1979) killed approximately 1.7 million people and nearly destroyed Cambodian Buddhism entirely. All religious practices were banned; monks who gave sermons on the First Precept ("do not kill") were targeted and often killed. Cambodians use the Pali-Sanskrit loan word *ukrittakamm* ("unbearable disaster") for the genocide.
**Academic Source:** Kittisenee (2024), pp. 517-519 (genocide details, destruction of Buddhism, the term ukrittakamm). The elephants' exodus of 1975 is documented on pp. 517-518 (Case Study 8).
**Game Event Source Field:** `Knowledge base: Cambodia -- Khmer Rouge genocide and near-destruction of Buddhism`
**Knowledge Base Trace:** Knowledge base entry draws from Kittisenee (2024), pp. 517-519.

### Adolescence

#### kh_adolescence_001: The Angkor Heritage Trip
**Cultural Practice:** The Angkor complex contains both Hindu (Angkor Wat) and Buddhist (the Bayon) monuments. The great Khmer kings practiced both Hindu and Buddhist religion, patron of divine kingship (*devaraja*) and Buddhist wisdom. The Bayon's enormous stone faces represent Avalokiteshvara, the Bodhisattva of Compassion.
**Academic Source:** Rush (2018), Chapter 2: "Kingdoms," discusses Angkor's dominance (800s-1400s) and the Hindu-Buddhist synthesis. Kittisenee (2024), pp. 515-517 (Angkor's Buddhist heritage). Blackburn (2012) discusses the textual and monumental traditions of Southeast Asian Buddhism.
**Game Event Source Field:** `Knowledge base: Cambodia -- Angkor complex, Hindu-Buddhist heritage`
**Knowledge Base Trace:** Knowledge base entry draws from Rush (2018), Ch. 2 and Kittisenee (2024), pp. 515-517.

#### kh_adolescence_002: The Pagoda Youth Group
**Cultural Practice:** Post-genocide, Cambodian young people face the challenge of rebuilding Buddhist knowledge that was nearly erased under the Khmer Rouge. Youth groups at pagodas teach meditation, Buddhist ethics, and community service. The Khmer Rouge "replaced the central faith that children should trust their parents" with ideology viewing the "old generation" as obstacles.
**Academic Source:** Kittisenee (2024), pp. 518-519 (Khmer Rouge destruction of Buddhist knowledge and intergenerational trust).
**Game Event Source Field:** `Knowledge base: Cambodia -- rebuilding Buddhist institutions and identity after genocide`
**Knowledge Base Trace:** Knowledge base entry draws from Kittisenee (2024), pp. 518-519.

### Young Adulthood

#### kh_youngadult_001: The Peace Walk
**Cultural Practice:** The Dhammayietra ("pilgrimage for truth") peace walks, founded by Maha Ghosananda (1913-2007), traversed Cambodia from 1992 to 2018. ## Vietnam (12 Events)

**Cultural Practice:** The *ban tho* (ancestor altar) is found in virtually every Vietnamese home regardless of religious affiliation. Elevated on a table or shelf, it displays photos of the deceased, incense, vases of flowers, and fruit offerings. Offerings are made on the 1st and 15th of each lunar month with the deceased's favorite foods.
