## A. COUNTRY PROFILES

### LAOS (Boute 2024)

**Political Context & How It Shapes Buddhist Life:**
- Laos is a multi-ethnic nation where Buddhism coexists with diverse ethnic practices. In northern Laos (Phongsaly province, Boun Tay district), Tai Lue and Phounoy ethnic groups practice Theravada Buddhism alongside local spirit traditions.
- The town of Boun Tay is historically a muang (small principality) from the Luang Prabang tradition. Multiple waves of migration since the 19th century have created a multiethnic community of approximately 3,000 people (Lue, Phounoy, Khmu, Akha).
- The Lao state recognized Boun Tay as a district in the 1990s, bringing civil servants and greater integration with national structures.

**Contemporary Issues:**
- The connection between the Buddhist calendar and the agricultural rice cycle is weakening. Increasing diversity in agricultural practices and employment in other sectors means offerings to ancestors are now made at convenient times (e.g., New Year celebrations) rather than at traditional harvest-linked dates (p. 506).
- The phrasat (palace offering) ceremony -- the definitive ceremony to establish the deceased as an ancestor -- is indispensable but extremely costly. Some families wait many years to afford it. In one case, an elderly couple's son struggles with drug addiction, so the community expects grandchildren will have to carry out the ceremony (p. 507).

**Modernization & Changing Practice:**
- Cash crops (rubber, beans) are now sold to Chinese entrepreneurs, shifting the subsistence rice economy.
- Funerary costs are significant -- one funeral required 150 kg of pork and a dozen ducks. Financial contributions from villagers are meticulously recorded (p. 495).
- Despite Buddhist emphasis on non-self, local conceptions of multiple soul-essences (khwan, winnan) persist, varying by locality (p. 492).

**Gender & Monasticism:**
- Funerary labor is gendered: women tend to the deceased's appearance and prepare meals; men construct the coffin and gather wood for the cremation pyre (p. 495).
- At a relative's funeral, sons or grandsons may be temporarily ordained as monks as a way of transferring merit to the deceased (p. 509, note 5).

### MYANMAR / KAREN STATE (Chambers 2024)

**Political Context & How It Shapes Buddhist Life:**
- The setting is Hpa-an, Karen State, southeastern Myanmar -- a frontier region shaped by decades of armed conflict between the Myanmar military (Tatmadaw), Karen armed organizations, and militia groups.
- Between 2011 and 2020, sovereignty was "splintered" among ethnic armed organizations, local militias, Border Guard Forces (BGF), and the state military in a "mosaic of territorial control" (p. 48).
- Former DKBA (Democratic Karen Buddhist Army) commanders, now BGF officers, own the most profitable industries: hotels, mining, tourism, trade, real estate, rubber, cement, automobiles, construction, and transport (p. 62).
- Charismatic monks like U Thuzana played key roles in Plong communities and in legitimizing lay power structures, including building pagodas in Christian and Muslim compounds as part of Buddhist revivalism (p. 48).

**Contemporary Issues:**
- Buddhism is the "sonic tapestry of everyday life" -- sermons broadcast from monasteries are the first thing heard in the morning, from radios, TVs, smartphones, in homes, businesses, restaurants, and paddy fields (p. 49).
- Buddhist sermons, DVDs of sermons, and lay ascetics calling for dana from megaphones punctuate daily commercial life in the marketplace (p. 49).
- The "merit-power nexus": power and authority in Myanmar are deeply rooted in merit-making and related to wealth and social status. "Merit is power" -- merit begets social power and status, which in turn begets more merit (p. 59).

**Morality, Ethics & Daily Life:**
- The Plong Karen concept of thout kyar ("keeping faithful") is a core moral ideal that encompasses being faithful to oneself, others, community, and the land. It is grounded in the traditional practice of paddy cultivation and communal reciprocity (p. 64-67).
- Money earned from "unclean" (ma than shin te paik san) sources -- gambling, drug sales, debt collection -- is considered dangerous and morally polluting. "If you make dirty money, bad things can happen to you and your family" (p. 65).
- The concept of samma-ajiva (right livelihood) from Buddhist ethics maps onto local Plong concerns about unclean wealth (p. 65).
- Moral evaluation is complex: the wealthy "strongman" Saw Eh Klein is simultaneously praised as a saboung kachar (generous benefactor) for his lavish donations to monks and monasteries, and critiqued for his involvement in gambling, drugs, and predatory lending (p. 56-69).

**Gender in Buddhist Practice:**
- Women are commonly acknowledged as the principal supporters of the Sangha and given a special place as mothers. Both ordinary women and women of note are "dedicated patrons of Buddhism" through feeding monks, presenting cloth, and building monasteries (p. 58).
- Women's everyday participation in dana rituals is seen as an extension of domestic and maternal responsibilities. The lives of Buddhist women revolve around monthly Buddhist festivals and everyday donations of money or labor to support local monasteries (p. 58).
- Women are believed to need to "do more" spiritually to progress, as being born a woman is seen as evidence of an inadequate store of merit from past lives. Yet daily donations to the Sangha, which women often lead, provide opportunities to cultivate moral agency (p. 57).

**Spirits & the Supernatural:**
- Spirits are a ubiquitous feature of the Plong imaginary. Local spirits (tutelary spirits of the household) help with daily concerns but are easily offended. Ancestral spirits of parents or grandparents are honored during New Year to ensure protection (p. 68).
- Spirits and Buddhist religious monuments coexist in a symbiotic relationship -- both signify the importance of everyday moral practice for present and future lives (p. 68).
- U Thuzana was widely believed to possess supernatural powers (iddhi) and his pictures were used as a form of protection. He also gained a reputation for predicting winning lottery numbers, which he would encode in Buddhist terminology and numerology during sermons (p. 51, 54-55).

### THAILAND (Kidpromma & Taylor 2024)

**Political Context & How It Shapes Buddhist Life:**
- Thailand's reformed monastic tradition limits full ordination (upasamapada) to males who declare themselves as male and "not a so-called 'defective' man" (p. 177). This effectively bars transgender persons (katoey) from full bhikkhu ordination.
- The Thai Sangha operates under the ecclesiastical Thammayut Nikaya and Mahanikai frameworks, with formal hierarchies supervised by senior abbots.
- Historical center-nation-state early 20th century sangha reforms have shaped Thai religion, creating rigid gender binaries in monastic life (p. 176).

**Contemporary Issues:**
- Transgender persons (katoey) are looked down upon by both the sangha and lay community. They are perceived as "Other" who transgress traditional gender boundaries. The mainstream view is that their femininity results from bad karma (p. 173).
- Some katoey become Buddhist nuns (mae chii), donning white robes and observing eight or ten precepts. In formal bhikkhu settings, mae chii tend to live "rather ambiguous and frequently subservient lives" (p. 174).
- A beauty pageant winner had to remove implanted silicone breast to conform to monastic rules and become a monk (p. 177).
- Some ladyboy/men monks could not completely discard their "ladyboy-ness" or feminine sexuality, becoming a locus for criticism within the sangha (p. 177).
- Gay monks are assigned to clerical tasks, serving high-ranking monks, and other nurturing roles. Lay people and temples generally do not take gay monks as a problem or destroyers of Buddhism (p. 177).

**Gender, Sexuality & Monasticism:**
- The Pali Vinaya identifies four genders: male (purisa), female (itthi), inter-sex (ubhatovyanjanaka/ubhatobyañjanaka), and pandaka. There are five types of pandaka in extra-canonical literature (p. 177).
- The term pandaka is problematic and misinterpreted -- in Thai social context it refers to a eunuch and a man who prefers same-sex relations, though katoey (men who are excessively feminine) is the commonly used term (p. 177).
- Families of katoey who seek ordination often support it because ordination of a son generates merit for the family, especially the mother. "Stopping a person from ordination is believed to cause bad karma" (p. 177).
- Ordination options limit katoey to either: (a) discard feminine identity and ordain as a male bhikkhu, or (b) take eight/ten precepts as mae chii in a subordinate position, or (c) create a "third way" alternative like Khun Mae Tritrinn's monastery.

**The Case Study of Khun Mae Tritrinn:**
- Khun Mae (Mother Teacher) Tritrinn is a transgendered monastic teacher in northern Thailand who established Dantham Mahamongkon Paiboonpoonsuk -- a nunnery in Chiang Dao, Chiang Mai, specifically as a "third-way" monastic space.
- Born in North-eastern Thailand, she identified as a "daughter" (looksao) of the family from childhood. She has a petite feminine body, no beard or body hair (p. 179).
- She never conceals her gender identity but acknowledges she is not a woman. She identifies as katoey but takes eight precepts as mae chii to maintain her femininity (p. 179).
- She taught meditation in Central Europe for many years. After her adopted mother died and a relationship failed, she returned to Thailand and established Dantham in 2018 (p. 179).
- There are 8-12 mae chii and lay practitioners at Dantham, some transgendered, others women (p. 179).
- She kept a vow of silence for a year (originally planned for three years) and practiced cave meditation, following the example of Khruba Bunchum, a famous ascetic Shan/Tai Yuan monk (p. 180).
- Upon emerging from isolation, villagers noted "her skin was so bright and light" -- interpreted as spiritual achievement (p. 180).
- She uses Facebook as main channel for teaching dhamma, with 429,000 followers and 30-50 dedicated patrons (p. 182).
- Her followers are mostly women and katoey. Some nun disciples were awarded by Miss Tiffany Universe Thailand (a transgender beauty pageant) (p. 182).
- She practices Mano-mayiddhi meditation (mind-made magical prowess) from the lineage of Luang Phor Ruesi Ling Dam, and performs prosperity rituals, chants mantras, and provides sacred water (nam mon) to disciples (p. 181).
- She took a Bodhisattva vow: "Come home my dear children... I will be reborn again and again until I achieve Buddhahood" (p. 181).

### CAMBODIA (Kittisenee 2024)

**Political Context & How It Shapes Buddhist Life:**
- Cambodia's forests bear witness to the country's traumatic history: colonialism, civil wars, the Khmer Rouge genocide (1975-1979), and post-conflict deforestation.
- The Khmer Rouge regime killed approximately 1.7 million people. Buddhism and all religious practices were banned. Monks who gave sermons on the First Precept (abstinence from killing) were targeted. The central faith that children should trust their parents was replaced by ideology viewing the "old generation" as obstacles to progress (p. 518-519).
- French colonial presence from 1863 disturbed forests and mined mountains for gemstones. The colonial government granted concessions to private foreign companies (p. 517).
- The modern Buddhist reformed movement in Cambodia was initiated by the French colonial intent to "civilize" indigenous religion -- they wanted Buddhism to refrain from "mysticism, superstition, and esoteric practice" and conform to Western, Christian standards. This led to the Buddhist Institute being established in 1930 (p. 515).
- In 1942, a monastic uprising against colonial rule in Phnom Penh was brutally suppressed. Monks were prosecuted for chanting Pali verses as "mantras of protection" (p. 515).
- Post-genocide, deforestation continued as the government cut forests to deny Khmer Rouge forces refuge, and opportunist transnational logging businesses exacerbated destruction (p. 519).

**Contemporary Issues:**
- The Dhammayietra (pilgrimage for truth / peace walk) is Cambodia's most significant Buddhist peace movement. Founded by Maha Ghosananda (1913-2007), the walks traversed the country from 1992 to 2018 (p. 512).
- Forest ordination (tying monastic robes to trees) has become a key environmental practice. Trees wrapped in robes are considered sacred Buddhist monks -- "not to be killed or destroyed" (p. 520).
- In 2006, robes tied to trees at Preah Vihear were taken by villagers and worn as amulets for protection against landmines (p. 520).
- The walks deliberately traversed landmined areas as an act of Buddhist mindfulness -- "the idea of mindful walking came from the very fact that the participants walked on routes filled with landmines and unexploded ordinances" (p. 519).

**Environmental Buddhism:**
- "Our Dhammayietra office is anywhere under a tree" (p. 512).
- Maha Ghosananda: "We Buddhists must find the courage to leave our temples and enter the temples of human experience, temples that are filled with suffering... the refugee camps, the prisons, the ghettos, and the battlefields will then become our temples" (p. 515).
- Syvorn (successor to Ghosananda) extended the First Precept to deforestation: "When we teach the First Precept -- abstinence from killing -- we could also say, do not 'kill' the forest too" (p. 520).
- Buddhadasa Bhikkhu's influence: Buddhists live in interdependence with others, including nature. "Destroying the balance of nature means destroying our own lives. Preserving ecology means conserving dhamma as dhamma is our inner ecology" (p. 521).

**Khmer Cosmology & Spirits:**
- Neak Ta is the guardian spirit in Khmer tradition, protector of the earth and water, with ruling power over all living beings in forests and urban areas. Shrines to Neak Ta are found even within Buddhist pagoda precincts (p. 514).
- Prei (forest/jungle) is "horrifying, unknowable, and undesirable" in Khmer perception, while Srok (village/urban) is "where things are supposed to be in order." The most vengeful ghost in Khmer animist tradition is Preay, whose pronunciation is close to Prei (p. 513).
- March walks coincide with the Khmer practice of bangsokol -- merit-making for deceased relatives who "come back and wander the earth for merit" before Khmer New Year in April (p. 523).

### VIETNAM (Nguyen 2024)

**Political Context & How It Shapes Buddhist Life:**
- Vietnam has a complex Buddhist history shaped by colonialism, war, and communist rule. The Vietnam Buddhist Reform movement began in the 1920s as a response to Western colonialism and Christian proselytization (p. 392).
- During the 1920s-1970s, Vietnamese Buddhist reformers created Buddhist journals, schools (kindergartens to university level), youth groups, and social welfare institutions including orphanages (p. 392).
- Two well-known youth organizations: Gia dinh Phat tu (Vietnam Buddhist Youth Family) with 70,000 members and 3,000 leaders by the 1960s, and the School of Youth for Social Services founded by Thich Nhat Hanh in 1965 (p. 392).
- After the Vietnam War ended in 1975, the communist government shut down most Buddhist programs following "atheist secularism." Buddhist activities were limited to small-scale household chanting by older women (p. 392-393).
- Economic reforms (Doi Moi) in 1986 and the establishment of the National Vietnam Buddhist Sangha in 1981 allowed Buddhist activities to grow again. The private economy also brought "new forms of socioeconomic anxieties and precarity" that drove increased religious activity (p. 393).

**Contemporary Issues:**
- Vietnamese Buddhism is "mostly devotional" -- lay Buddhists go to temples on the 1st and 15th of the lunar month to make offerings to buddhas and bodhisattvas, pray for well-being, academic success, romantic partnership, etc. (p. 391).
- Among most lay Buddhists, there is no strict distinction between Buddhist figures and other spirits and deities. Chanting liturgical texts in Sino-Vietnamese and transliterated Sanskrit -- even without comprehension -- is believed to generate good merits (p. 391).
- Since the mid-2000s there has been a proliferation of Buddhist youth programs: weekend retreats, weekly classes, meditation programs, summer camps, and volunteering initiatives (p. 393).
- Buddhist temples now offer Buddhist wedding ceremonies -- a "relatively new phenomenon in Vietnam" designed partly to attract young people (p. 394).

**Youth Engagement:**
- At Giac Ngo (Enlightenment) temple in HCMC, weekend retreats for young children attracted 500+ attendees. The temple also offered Vipassana meditation, Thich Nhat Hanh's Plum Village mindfulness, and volunteering activities (p. 394).
- The abbot identifies "Dharma illiteracy" (mu chu Phat phap) as a key problem -- the dominant focus on devotional and supplication practices has alienated young people, professionals, and intellectuals from Buddhism (p. 394).
- Two contrasting approaches: Giac Ngo temple (modernist -- Buddhism as ethical/philosophical tradition, textual learning, meditation) vs. Temple of Harmony (combining devotional Mahayana practices with modernist elements) (p. 397-398).
- Young people are drawn to Buddhism for diverse reasons: philosophical interest, therapeutic dimensions (coping with psychosocial problems and anxieties), social networking, career opportunities, and the concept of duyen (predestined karmic affinity) with a temple (p. 399-400).
- Youth participation fluctuates -- high during university years, declining when starting jobs or families, returning during periods of existential crisis (p. 400).
- Young Vietnamese also combine Buddhism with tarot card reading, astrology, fortune telling, popular psychology tests, and self-improvement courses in their "projects of self-cultivation" (p. 400-401).
- The "three steps, one prostration" (tam bo nhat bai) practice from East Asian Mahayana and Tibetan Buddhism was incorporated into retreats -- explained both devotionally (repenting wrong deeds, shedding bad karma) and from a health perspective (blood circulation, physical exercise) (p. 396-397).

**Modernism vs. Devotionalism:**
- Class distinction in Buddhist approach: middle/upper-middle-class, well-educated youth are more attracted to modernist Buddhism (compatible with science and psychology); less educated youth may retain devotional practices (p. 399).
- Trinh, a well-educated businesswoman, preferred the modernist focus on individual cultivation and found devotional supplication to buddhas and deities incongruent with her modern education (p. 399).
- Hang, a young rural migrant, combined Vipassana meditation, mindfulness, and praying to the Bodhisattva of Compassion, alongside tarot cards and astrology (p. 399-400).

### REGIONAL OVERVIEW (RelSEA Slides)

**Mandala Political Formations (Pre-colonial):**
- Southeast Asian polities before colonialism were organized as mandalas: dynamic, competitive political formations with central power and local elites in hierarchical patron-client relationships.
- Key mandala centers: Pagan/Burma, Ayutthaya/Siam, Angkor/Cambodia, Champa, Sri Vijaya, Majapahit/Java, Brunei, Melaka, Sulu.
- Characterized by: investment in monumental architecture and religious devotion infrastructure, dependence on agriculture and high-value natural resources, and riverine/maritime trade.

**Key Historical Facts:**
- Scholars group SE Asian communities by "upland hills and lowland plains."
- Japan occupied much of SE Asia during WWII.
- ASEAN was NOT established primarily as a security alliance.
- 11 nations make up contemporary Southeast Asia.
- Thailand was the only SE Asian kingdom to avoid overt colonialism.

**Transmissions of Religion:**
- Religions themselves do not have agency (avoid "Buddhism does..." statements).
- Mutually exclusive, confessional religious identities are a recent development.
- Devotional practices include local spirits/deities associated with particular places, selectively adapted within trans-local religious systems.
- Religions and religious identities are not monolithic; they are internally diverse and contested.

**Key Sites:**
- Borobudur (Central Java, 9th century, Sailendra Dynasty) -- monumental Buddhist architecture.
- Pagan (Central Myanmar, 9th-13th centuries) -- Buddhist temples and murals.
- Angkor Wat (Siem Reap, northeast Cambodia, 12th century) -- built under Khmer empire Suryavarman II, rededicated as Buddhist site under Jayavarman VII and Indradevi.

## B. BUDDHIST CONCEPTS & PRACTICES

### Contemporary Buddhism: How Traditional Practices Adapt to Modern Life

**Merit-Making & Dana (Giving):**
- Dana (Pali: "gift") produces good karmic consequences and is inseparable from Theravada devotion. Making merit through gifts to the Sangha -- building shrines, supporting festivals, giving alms -- is "generally considered the most important means through which people gain access to spiritual power" (Chambers, p. 57).
- In Myanmar, dana can refer to a wide range of good deeds: charity to the poor, giving food, hospitality. The monastic donation economy creates a "merit-power nexus" linking merit, wealth, and social status (Chambers, p. 59).
- For women, everyday acts of merit-making (morning almsgiving, festival preparation, food offering) provide spiritual opportunities and also reinforce exemplary qualities as mothers (Chambers, p. 57).
- In Vietnam, central to devotional practices is the framework of merit-making where supplication to buddhas, bodhisattvas, and Dharma protectors, plus charitable donations, generate good merits for oneself and family (Nguyen, p. 391).

**Karma & Moral Selfhood:**
- In Karen Plong Buddhism, moral subjectivity is "based on the sum of all deeds performed, both in this and in previous lives" (K. khoung). The individual self is constituted by accumulated merit and good deeds (Chambers, p. 56).
- Parami (P. parami) is used among Plong Buddhists to refer to virtue or aptitude toward enlightenment. The ten principle virtues are: charity, morality, renunciation, knowledge, effort, honesty, forbearance, loving-kindness, equanimity, and resolution (Chambers, p. 52).
- In Vietnam, duyen (predestined karmic affinity) is used by young Buddhists to explain why they feel drawn to particular temples or teachers -- understood as "consequences of past-life relationships which cause predestined inclinations toward people, places, things, and preferences in this life" (Nguyen, p. 400).

**Buddhist Modernism vs. Devotionalism:**
- In Vietnam, two distinct "forces" shape Buddhist practice: modernist Buddhism (philosophical, ethical, meditation-focused, compatible with science) and devotional Buddhism (supplication to buddhas/bodhisattvas/spirits, chanting, merit-making rituals) (Nguyen, p. 393).
- These are not mutually exclusive sects but rather "flows" or "forces" that coexist within the same temples and individuals (Nguyen, p. 393).
- Buddhist modernism in Vietnam dates to the 1920s Buddhist Reform movement, partly a response to colonialism and Christian proselytization (Nguyen, p. 392).

### Environmental Buddhism & Forest Monasteries

**Forests as Sacred Buddhist Space:**
- Throughout Buddhist history, specific types of trees and forests are central to spiritual experience. The Buddha's life history is intimately connected to forests: born in a garden under the Sala tree, enlightened under the Bodhi tree at age 35, first sermon in Isipatana forest, first monastery in a bamboo forest, passed away in a forest at Kushinagar (Kittisenee, p. 512-513).
- Forest meditation monks (araññavasi, "forest dwellers") use the untamed wilderness to tame the mind. "Encountering evil spirits, wild elephants, tigers, unidentified corpses, and battling with sexual desire in the forest are integral parts in the Buddhist religious experience" (Kittisenee, p. 513).
- In Khmer (Cambodian) society, the forest (Prei) has a distinct cultural meaning: "horrifying yet full of power" vs. the village (Srok): "urban, livable space... where things are supposed to be in order" (Kittisenee, p. 513).

**Dhammayietra Peace Walks:**
- Founded by Maha Ghosananda (1913-2007), a student of reformist patriarch Samdech Chuon Nath. Ghosananda spoke 15 languages and was influenced by Gandhi's satyagraha and Japanese Buddhist monk Nichidatsu Fujii's anti-atomic bomb activism (Kittisenee, p. 516).
- The first Dhammayietra in 1992 was a 430 km walk from Poipet (Thai-Cambodian border) to Phnom Penh, starting at Site II, the biggest refugee camp (Kittisenee, p. 516).
- Local people related Ghosananda to the Buddhist prophecy of Preah Bat Dhammik, "the meritorious-charismatic leader" who would come from the west to heal Cambodia (Kittisenee, p. 516).
- In 1994, crossfire during the third peace walk between government forces and Khmer Rouge resulted in the death of two pilgrims, a monk and a nun (Kittisenee, p. 516).

**Tree/Forest Ordination:**
- Originated in northern Thailand in the late 1980s by Buddhist monks working with community residents. By tying a monastic robe to a tree, the tree is "considered as a sacred Buddhist monk -- thus, it is meant not to be killed or destroyed" (Kittisenee, p. 520).
- Contributed to the environmental movement across mainland SE Asia in the late 1980s-1990s. Motivated by concern that modern development, industrialization, and consumerism have destroyed natural resources (Kittisenee, p. 521).
- Buddhadasa Bhikkhu, an influential Thai reformed Buddhist thinker, affirmed that Buddhists live in interdependence with nature: "Preserving ecology means conserving dhamma as dhamma is our inner ecology" (Kittisenee, p. 521).

### Youth Engagement with Buddhism

**Vietnam:**
- Buddhist summer camps, weekly classes, weekend retreats, meditation programs, and volunteering initiatives have proliferated since the mid-2000s (Nguyen, p. 393).
- Youth programs are designed to attract youth interest, encourage Buddhist identity adoption, and help navigate socioeconomic and psychosocial challenges (Nguyen, p. 393).
- Youth involvement is high during university years; decreases when starting jobs/families; returns during existential crises (Nguyen, p. 400).
- Social media is key: temples use Facebook and social media pages for announcements, promotion, and content sharing (Nguyen, p. 395).

**Myanmar:**
- U Thuzana's sermons warned young Karen that "all the young people these days are forgetting about their culture and religion. They have boyfriends or girlfriends and they drink and take drugs." He urged keeping traditional customs and the Buddhist religion alive (Chambers, p. 54).

**Thailand:**
- Khun Mae Tritrinn uses Facebook live broadcasts and daily dhamma talks streamed online to reach 429,000 followers, with 30-50 dedicated patrons who are the main supporters through mutual connections, friends, and online media (Kidpromma, p. 182).

### Ancestor Veneration & Funerary Practices

**Laos Funerary Process (Boute 2024) -- Detailed Multi-Day Sequence:**

*Day 1 - Song Sakan ("Sending Away the Corpse"):*
- Death announced over village loudspeaker; village districts collect 15,000 kips (USD 1) per family for the bereaved (p. 495).
- Body is washed, dressed in new clothes with make-up applied. Women cover eyes with a small towel and wrap body in white cloth. A small amount of money placed in pocket/purse/hand (p. 495).
- The acan (lay leader, a former monk) determines the auspicious cremation date from the calendar (p. 495).
- Six young monks arrive to offer prayers. The house transforms into huean di ("house of joy") -- "the home of someone who has departed from life; it signifies the cessation of suffering in existence" (p. 496).
- Bamboo trellises (taleo) placed at four corners of cremation pyre to deter malevolent spirits (p. 496).
- Sompoi water (containing Acacia concinna pods, blessed by monks) is used for purification throughout (p. 496).

*Day 2 - Cremation:*
- Sut Kusala prayer read for the deceased: "during our existence, we may have done both good and evil, and that our existence was marked by suffering" (p. 496).
- The deceased's husband severs a cotton thread connecting him to the coffin -- symbolizing severed ties. "On the one hand, it's so that the souls of the dead go to heaven; on the other, the husband cuts it to show his ties with the dead woman are broken, as if they were divorcing" (p. 497).
- A soul-recalling ritual is performed with a fish trap to retrieve souls of the living that may have been in contact with the deceased (p. 497).
- Raw egg thrown to select the location for the reliquary: "if it's not the right place, the egg won't break" (p. 497).
- After cremation, attendees wash at the riverbank. Hair washing is forbidden until after the funeral proper (p. 497).
- Bones are washed until white, placed in sompoi water, dried, perfumed, wrapped in cloth, and placed in a funeral urn. "Bone money" (ngueune du) is placed inside -- "for the deceased's journey to paradise (muang savane)" (p. 498).

*Final Day - Feeding and Expelling the Dead:*
- The acan begins prayers in Lao, recalling the deceased's name and day of death, addressing the deceased to come take possession of offerings (p. 498).
- Libations poured as medium for merit transfer: "Until this is done, the deceased cannot take possession of it" (p. 498).
- The text Sut Sang Kara (comprising Sut Karani and Sut Sakhanitsa) is read to compel the spirit to depart. Family sits in center of room encircled by cotton thread (p. 498-499).
- The last prayer is Sut Mangkala, a prayer of protection and blessing (p. 499).
- Monks throw stones with sompoi water onto roofs to scare away spirits. Mak kua (thorny eggplant) leaves sprinkled at four corners. Bamboo trellises placed at house corners with cotton thread. Family washes hair: "This is how you wash away all impurities and are reborn as it were, a new person" (p. 500).

**The Phrasat ("Palace") Offering Ceremony:**
- The ultimate offering to definitively establish the deceased as an ancestor, held months or years after the funeral (p. 503).
- A miniature house (phrasat, from Pali prasada) is built at the temple, spacious enough for an adult, containing everything the deceased might need: mat, mattress, pillow, clothing, kitchen utensils, notebooks, pens, soap, sweets, cookies, and banknotes (p. 504).
- Offerings ranked in ascending importance: bamboo trunks with flowers/silver, a flag, a miniature rice granary, and a small house. The palace is the most important (p. 503).
- Six prayers recited by monks: (1) confirming all belongings are present; (2) inspiring stories encouraging the deceased to take ownership; (3) blessings for everyone; (4) presenting miniature rice granary; (5) sending the "palace"; (6) final offerings and apologies for any unintentional disrespect to the Buddha (p. 505).
- "You cannot skip phrasat." Even those who lack financial means wait years or transfer the responsibility to children/grandchildren (p. 507).
- Until phrasat is offered, the deceased may manifest in dreams, observe during prayers, or cause disturbances. After phrasat, the dead "will no longer need anything. Where he is, he will have everything" (p. 507).

**Multiple Soul Concepts:**
- Deceased persons are not thought of as having a single principle but rather multiple elements: khwan (vital essences, variable in number -- 9, 32, etc., sometimes associated with body parts) and winnan (a more impermanent principle that outlives the deceased and transmigrates) (p. 492).
- Loss of some khwan causes illness and discomfort; loss of all results in death. Rituals to recall these essences are conducted by laypeople to ensure well-being (p. 492).
- After death, souls can reside in different locations: at home, in the cemetery, in paradise. They can be avoided or summoned to different places at specific times (p. 492).
- Among the Phounoy, nine souls of a living person are distributed in different places after death -- some in paradise, others in the cemetery, some in the home (p. 502).

### Transgender/Gender-Nonconforming Experiences in Buddhist Monasticism

**Vinaya Gender Categories:**
- The Pali Vinaya identifies four genders: male (purisa), female (itthi), intersex (ubhatovyanjanaka), and pandaka (Kidpromma, p. 177).
- Five types of pandaka in extra-canonical literature: (1) asitta-pandaka (fellatio), (2) usuya-pandaka (voyeur), (3) opakkamika-pandaka (eunuch/castrated), (4) pakkha-pandaka (half-time pandaka, only during waning moon), (5) napumsaka-pandaka (born without sexual organs, ambiguous genitalia) (Kidpromma, p. 177).

**Contemporary Thai Context:**
- The term katoey is synonymous with Tud and Taew (excessive female characteristics in a man) and is used alternately among Thais (Kidpromma, p. 177).
- Gay men are welcome in monasteries, but katoey, tud, and taew are usually discouraged from ordaining (Kidpromma, p. 177).
- Thai Theravada Buddhism is "biased, offering less religious space for non-straight male persons" and is used "to promote and maintain the idea of ideal man and a male dominant culture in Thailand" (Kidpromma, p. 177).

**Third-Way Monasticism:**
- Khun Mae Tritrinn's Dantham monastery represents a "third space" (following Soja's concept of Thirdspace): a socially produced space that is neither male nor female, neither orthodox nor heterodox, but a hybrid alternative (Kidpromma, p. 178).
- This third-way monasticism applies "acceptable norms and practices, motherly love and care, the bodhisattva ideal present in Thai Northern Buddhism, magic practices, and popular Buddhism" (Kidpromma, p. 183).
- The nunnery provides a place for "placeless" persons -- LGBTQIA+ community members and women "who are marginalised and unable to find comfort in conventional Buddhist monasteries" (Kidpromma, p. 183).
- When initially establishing the center, locals believed the katoey nun and followers would bring bad luck (khued) to the community. Some people chased them away during morning alms. "To get anything like 10,000 THB donation (about 300 USD) for the monastery is beyond a dream, and to be accepted by the locals almost impossible" (Kidpromma, p. 179).

## C. CASE STUDIES & REAL STORIES

### Case Study 1: The Funeral at Boun Tay (Boute 2024)
**Setting:** Boun Tay, Phongsaly Province, northern Laos, January 2023.
**Individual:** A 60-year-old woman who died of a heart attack.
**Key Details:**
- Left behind a husband, two sons, and three daughters. Extended family (sisters, brothers, spouses) manage the arrangements, not the immediate household -- they are experienced in these matters.
- The funeral required 150 kg of pork and a dozen ducks. Visitors bring soft drinks and sweets; some offer financial assistance recorded in a notebook.
- A low table with the deceased's favorite foods is placed beside the body throughout.
- The ceremony reveals the fundamental tension in Theravada Buddhism: between the doctrine that fate is determined by one's own actions and the widespread practice of transferring merit to the deceased.
- The deceased is considered a spirit (phi tay) who "does not understand what has happened to her and wishes to remain with her loved ones" but also "attracts all kinds of malevolent spirits" (p. 501).

### Case Study 2: The Phrasat Offering for a Young Man (Boute 2024)
**Setting:** Boun Tay temple, end of February 2023.
**Individual:** A man who passed away at age 34 the previous year.
**Dilemma:** His young wife wanted to remarry, which required severing ties with the deceased once and for all. Normally families wait many years to afford this costly ceremony, but the remarriage need accelerated it. The responsibility fell on the deceased's elder brother (p. 504).
**Key Detail:** The joyous procession with gongs, chants, and shouting -- elderly men dancing with sabers -- contrasts with what is fundamentally a farewell ceremony. At the end, everyone moves hastily away from the altar "because at this point the dead man comes and goes one last time to come and take things" (p. 505).

### Case Study 3: Saw Eh Klein -- The Buddhist Strongman (Chambers 2024)
**Setting:** Hpa-an, Karen State, southeastern Myanmar, 2016.
**Individual:** Saw Eh Klein, father of Nan Eh Hti Paw (the author's university-aged friend).
**Dilemma:** Saw Eh Klein is simultaneously one of the most generous Buddhist benefactors in the community (saboung kachar) and a former DKBA officer whose wealth derives from controlling gambling, drugs, and predatory lending. He handles 20 million kyat per week (USD 15,000) while average household income is 100,000-150,000 kyat per month (USD 80-120).
**Key Tensions:**
- His family's everyday acts of dana (daily alms, sponsoring novice ordinations, large festival donations) are genuinely praised.
- Elderly neighbor Hpi Ha Mya warns the author: "If you make dirty money, bad things can happen to you and your family... Ma than shin te paik san, unclean money, is not the habit of Plong people" (p. 65).
- A villager who sold drugs and was deeply indebted to Saw Eh Klein was believed to have been possessed by a powerful spirit and hanged himself in the cemetery. A university student noted: "That's what happens when you do unclean business" (p. 67).
- Despite all this, Saw Eh Klein and his wife "always look after people from our village... they have thout kyar" (p. 64).

### Case Study 4: U Thuzana -- The Charismatic Monk (Chambers 2024)
**Setting:** Myaing Gyi Ngu, Karen State, Myanmar.
**Individual:** U Thuzana, a powerful charismatic Karen Buddhist monk, patron of the DKBA.
**Key Details:**
- Widely believed to possess supernatural powers (iddhi). His pictures used as protective amulets.
- Connected to oxygen tank, breathing heavily, surrounded by devotees including his close associate (a local armed BGF commander) who fanned his body.
- His sermons mixed classical moral exhortation (keep thila, do dana) with Karen ethno-nationalism (guard against "Islamisation," preserve Plong customs and traditions), and practical lottery number predictions encoded in Buddhist numerology.
- He joked: "It is good that so many Plong people gamble on the two-digit lottery so that I can continue to keep building as many pagodas as possible" (p. 54).
- He passed away in November 2018.

### Case Study 5: Khun Mae Tritrinn's Journey (Kidpromma 2024)
**Setting:** Dantham, Chiang Dao, Chiang Mai, northern Thailand.
**Individual:** Khun Mae (Mother Teacher) Tritrinn, a transgendered monastic teacher.
**Journey:**
- Born in North-eastern Thailand, identified as female (looksao) from childhood.
- Taught meditation in Central Europe for many years.
- After adopted mother's death and a failed relationship, returned to Thailand.
- Took eight-preceptor ordination at a well-known Chiang Dao monastery and practiced meditation for several months.
- Experienced insight visions (nimitta) that led her to a specific mountain location.
- Purchased land from villagers with her own money and labor; established Dantham in 2018.
- Faced severe community rejection: people chased her away during alms rounds, "as if we were animals."
- Met the famous ascetic monk Khruba Bunchum, who gave her a northern Thai religious hat, a mala bead, and 5,000 baht. The mala bead means "Sila, Samadhi, Panna, the path to Buddhahood and 5000 baht is for helping humanities and distribute the Buddha dhamma for 5000 years" (p. 180).
- Followed Khruba Bunchum's example of cave retreat: kept silence for one year, ate only roots and vegetables. Broke the vow due to COVID-19 (p. 180).
- After emerging, gained eventual acceptance from the community. "When Khun Mae came out from the cave, her skin was so bright and light" -- a sign of spiritual attainment (p. 180).
- Now has 429,000 Facebook followers and her center is expanding with support from educated, middle-class disciples.

### Case Study 6: Mali's Mother and the Khmer Rouge (Kittisenee 2024)
**Setting:** Northwestern Cambodia, during the Pol Pot regime.
**Individual:** Mali's mother, forced into labor under the Khmer Rouge.
**Story:** Starving to death, she asked Khmer Rouge cadres for a plate of rice. Instead of refusing, they brought her ten huge trays of steamed rice and forced her to finish them all or die. "Between a rock and a hard place, between starving to death or being murdered by the cadres for challenging their order, my mom put up her palms and prayed to the forest. She then made an upakit, taking a sacred vow. If the dhamma and the sacred beings prevailed in that forest, may the power of them bestow upon her, enabling her to finish all the rice. Nobody believed my mom would be able to do that, but she did. Even the Khmer Rouge cadres were surprised and let her go" (p. 520).
**Significance:** The forest as a source of dhamma, refuge, hope, and miraculous power even during genocide.

### Case Study 7: Hang's Buddhist Self-Cultivation (Nguyen 2024)
**Setting:** Ho Chi Minh City, contemporary Vietnam.
**Individual:** Hang, a young rural migrant woman in her early thirties (composite character).
**Dilemma:** Came to HCMC from a rural area for higher education, hoping to secure a well-paying job. Family wanted her to get married and settle down; she wanted to find her calling. Torn between different life trajectories.
**Resolution:** Turned to Vipassana meditation, mindfulness practices, and praying to the Bodhisattva of Compassion. Also consulted tarot cards, astrology, and self-improvement courses. Found communities of support at Giac Ngo and Temple of Harmony through ethics classes and meditation groups (p. 399-400).

### Case Study 8: The Elephants' Exodus (Kittisenee 2024)
**Setting:** Eastern Cambodia, 1975.
**Story told by:** Syvorn, a Dhammayietra leader.
**Account:** A large herd of elephants that once lived across several Cambodian provinces (Prey Veng, Kampong Cham, Kratie, Mondulkiri, Stung Treng, Ratanakiri) suddenly moved northward into Laos and Thailand in 1975. When asked why, Syvorn replied: "It is said that they knew what was going on in the country, the ukrittakamm" -- a Pali-Sanskrit loan word meaning "unbearable disaster," referring to the Khmer Rouge genocide (p. 517-518).
**Significance:** The elephants' sensitivity to human moral catastrophe mirrors the Buddhist understanding that all sentient beings respond to moral and immoral conditions.

## D. ENCOUNTER SEEDS

### Encounter 1: The Funeral Dilemma (from Boute 2024)
**Situation:** A family member has died. You must organize the funeral, but keeping the body at home for more auspicious timing costs more (food for many visitors over multiple days).
**Choices:**
- (A) Wait for the most auspicious day (5 days) -- costs significantly more, but generates maximum merit and ensures proper passage
- (B) Cremate quickly (1-2 days) -- saves money but may not be the right calendar day
- (C) Ask the community to collect contributions from every family in the district
**What's at stake:** Family finances, the deceased's journey to paradise, community standing, the well-being of the living (their khwan must be protected from contact with death)

### Encounter 2: The Phrasat Obligation (from Boute 2024)
**Situation:** Years have passed since your parent's death. You cannot afford the phrasat (palace offering) ceremony, which is the definitive ritual to establish the deceased as an ancestor. Your deceased parent appears in dreams and on Buddhist holidays. Your sibling's spouse wants to remarry, which requires the phrasat.
**Choices:**
- (A) Take on debt to perform the ceremony now
- (B) Wait and continue making smaller offerings during Buddhist Lent holidays
- (C) Transfer the responsibility to your own children
**What's at stake:** The deceased's final resting status in the afterlife, family obligations and filial piety, financial stability, a sibling's future marriage prospects

### Encounter 3: The Strongman's Donation (from Chambers 2024)
**Situation:** A wealthy local figure -- who is known to control gambling and drug operations -- offers to sponsor a major pagoda celebration in your village and to fund the ordination of several local boys as novice monks.
**Choices:**
- (A) Accept the sponsorship gratefully -- his dana generates merit for everyone and supports the sasana
- (B) Politely decline -- "unclean money" (ma than shin te paik san) from gambling and drugs will bring bad karma
- (C) Accept but distance yourself from the sponsor personally -- attend the ceremony but don't become part of his patronage network
**What's at stake:** Community religious infrastructure, your moral standing (thout kyar), the risk of being drawn into debt/patronage, merit for the ordaining boys' families

### Encounter 4: The Lottery Sermon (from Chambers 2024)
**Situation:** A powerful charismatic monk is giving a sermon. At the end, he provides encoded lottery numbers during his Buddhist recitation. Your family is deeply in debt.
**Choices:**
- (A) Try to decipher the numbers and gamble -- "only those with the appropriate karma can find the winning number"
- (B) Focus only on the dhamma teachings and ignore the lottery aspect
- (C) Attend the sermon for the merit of showing devotion but avoid gambling entirely -- gambling contradicts Right Livelihood
**What's at stake:** Family debt, spiritual merit, the fine line between faith and gambling addiction, your relationship with karma

### Encounter 5: Transgender Ordination (from Kidpromma 2024)
**Situation:** You identify as katoey (transgender). You feel a strong calling to monastic life and spiritual practice. The village temple will not accept you.
**Choices:**
- (A) Suppress your gender identity and ordain as a male bhikkhu (227 rules) -- gaining full ordination status but denying yourself
- (B) Take eight precepts as a mae chii (nun) -- maintaining femininity but accepting subordinate, ambiguous status
- (C) Seek out a "third-way" monastery like Dantham that accepts transgender practitioners
- (D) Remain a dedicated lay practitioner and practice independently
**What's at stake:** Spiritual path, gender identity, community acceptance, family merit (ordination of a "son" sends parents to heaven), access to formal Buddhist institutional support

### Encounter 6: The Nunnery Under Siege (from Kidpromma 2024)
**Situation:** You've established a small meditation center as a transgender monastic teacher. The local community suspects you bring bad luck (khued). Villagers chase you away during morning alms. An influential royal temple demands you stop wearing saffron robes.
**Choices:**
- (A) Comply and switch to white mae chii robes -- maintain peace but lose symbolic equality
- (B) Keep wearing saffron and face continued conflict with the establishment
- (C) Switch to brown robes (of the reformed forest monk lineage) as a compromise
- (D) Move the center to a more remote location away from the village
**What's at stake:** Institutional legitimacy, community relations, self-expression, the survival of the center, the followers who depend on it

### Encounter 7: Forest vs. Development (from Kittisenee 2024)
**Situation:** A forested area near your village is scheduled for agricultural clearing. Monks have previously ordained the trees (tied monastic robes around them). Local developers want the land.
**Choices:**
- (A) Support the tree ordination and oppose clearing -- "abstinence from killing" extends to forests
- (B) Allow the clearing but ensure replanting elsewhere -- villagers need land for agriculture
- (C) Organize a Dhammayietra-style peace walk through the forest to raise awareness
- (D) Take the robes from the trees and use them as protective amulets (as villagers did at Preah Vihear)
**What's at stake:** Environmental preservation, livelihood needs of the community, Buddhist ethics (First Precept extended to nature), sacred space

### Encounter 8: The Peace Walk Through Landmines (from Kittisenee 2024)
**Situation:** You've been invited to join a Dhammayietra peace walk. The route passes through former conflict zones with possible unexploded ordinances. The walk is meant to be an act of mindful walking and Buddhist healing.
**Choices:**
- (A) Join the walk -- practice mindfulness literally, as the monks originally did on landmined paths
- (B) Join but insist on staying within roped-off safe areas
- (C) Support the walk financially and spiritually but don't physically participate
- (D) Volunteer to help with tree ordinations at the walk's conclusion
**What's at stake:** Personal safety, spiritual practice, contribution to post-conflict healing, merit-making for deceased relatives (walks coincide with bangsokol season)

### Encounter 9: Youth Buddhist Retreat (from Nguyen 2024)
**Situation:** You're a university student in Ho Chi Minh City. A temple offers a weekend retreat combining Dharma lectures, meditation, and a talk show with a successful celebrity businessperson. Your exams are next week.
**Choices:**
- (A) Attend the retreat -- spiritual cultivation is a form of "total life practice" that will help you in all areas
- (B) Skip the retreat and study -- practical obligations come first, you can attend next month
- (C) Attend only the meditation portion and skip the celebrity talk show
- (D) Go to the retreat and combine it with consulting a tarot reader about your exam results
**What's at stake:** Academic performance, spiritual growth, social networking (many young people find career opportunities at temple events), the duyen (karmic connection) with the temple community

### Encounter 10: Devotional vs. Modernist Buddhism (from Nguyen 2024)
**Situation:** Your grandmother wants you to come to the temple on the 1st of the lunar month to make offerings and pray to the Bodhisattva of Compassion for your family's health. Your modernist Buddhist friend says this is "superstitious" and that real Buddhism is about meditation and philosophical understanding.
**Choices:**
- (A) Go with your grandmother -- devotional practice has its own merit and strengthens family bonds
- (B) Go to the modernist temple's Vipassana session instead -- focus on meditation and ethical cultivation
- (C) Go with your grandmother but silently treat it as a meditation practice, reinterpreting the devotional elements through a modernist lens
- (D) Skip both and volunteer at the temple's charity organization instead
**What's at stake:** Family harmony, generational tensions about Buddhist practice, personal spiritual development, the relationship between merit-making and philosophical understanding

### Encounter 11: Dirty Money and Right Livelihood (from Chambers 2024)
**Situation:** In your village, a wealthy boss (thae hti) offers you a job managing his gambling and money-lending operation. The pay would be 10x what you earn farming rice. Your family is struggling financially.
**Choices:**
- (A) Accept the job -- you can use the money for dana and merit-making to offset the karma
- (B) Refuse -- "ma than shin te paik san" (dirty money) will bring suffering to you and your family
- (C) Accept temporarily to pay off debts, then return to farming
- (D) Ask the village monastery's monks for advice
**What's at stake:** Family survival, moral integrity (thout kyar), right livelihood (samma-ajiva), community reputation, risk of debt spirals and addiction

### Encounter 12: The Deceased's Spirit (from Boute 2024)
**Situation:** After your mother's funeral, strange things happen in the house. Objects move, family members feel unwell, you have vivid dreams of your mother asking for food and clothing.
**Choices:**
- (A) Contact the acan (lay leader) to perform a merit-transfer ceremony at the temple
- (B) Place daily food offerings at the household altar and address the deceased directly: "Eat, you've got everything you need... You mustn't disturb the villagers and your relatives"
- (C) Have monks throw sompoi water with river stones on the roof and place bamboo trellises at the four corners of the house
- (D) Begin saving for the phrasat ceremony to definitively settle the deceased in the afterlife
**What's at stake:** Family health and well-being, the deceased's spiritual journey, proper ritual obligations, the balance between mourning and moving on

**Key Concepts (Cross-cutting):**
- **Merit-power nexus** -- The convergence of merit-making, wealth, and political authority in Myanmar
- **Third-way monasticism** -- Alternative monastic path for gender-nonconforming persons (Thailand)
- **Thirdspace** -- Soja's concept of hybrid socially-produced space, applied to transgender monasticism
- **Mandala** -- Pre-colonial SE Asian political formation: dynamic, competitive, patron-client centered
- **Forest tradition vs. city tradition** -- Araññavasi (forest monks) vs. gamavasi (city monks)
- **Forest ordination** -- Tying monastic robes to trees as conservation practice
- **"Unclean money"** -- Moral concept that money from illicit sources carries negative karmic weight
