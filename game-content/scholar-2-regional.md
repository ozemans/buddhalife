## A. COUNTRY PROFILES

### Thailand (Siam)

**Political context and how it shapes Buddhist life:**
- Thailand is the only Southeast Asian kingdom to avoid overt colonialism (Rush, p.7). Cunning political actors dodged colonization by collaborating with Japan in WWII and aligning with the US during the Cold War.
- A "sometime democracy and oftentimes a military-led state," it coalesces around the memory of 19th-century kings and the still-revered royal family (Rush, p.7).
- In Thailand, virtually all elected and nonelected leaders pledge loyalty to the king. The governing class includes "conspicuously its power-seizing generals" (Rush, p.19).
- Thailand's national history has been the "official royal/national history" since the late 1970s, but has been shaken by alternative narratives. The golden age of Sukhothai was undermined when its famous inscription was said to have been written later than claimed (Thongchai, p.4).
- Thai studies in Thailand are shaped by "Buddhism, royalism, and Thai parochialism" as dominant intellectual traditions (Thongchai, p.21).
- Modern scholarship in Thailand was never dictated by a colonial power; colonial knowledge was imported and adapted to serve domestic interests since the late 19th century (Thongchai, p.21).

**Buddhist institutional structure:**
- The Shan community in Baan Kaung Mu (northern Thailand) demonstrates a village-level Buddhist structure where the monastery (*wat*) is the center of communal religious life (Eberhardt, Ch. 7).
- Temple sleepers (*khon naun tsaung* / *khon thao*) are a recognized social institution: elderly people who take up eight precepts and live at the temple (Eberhardt, Ch. 7, p.157).
- Lay readers perform scriptural readings at merit-making ceremonies while monks lead services (Eberhardt, Ch. 7, p.162).
- The Sangha is supported materially by the lay community, who provide food (last meal at 11:00 AM), shelter, and offerings (Eberhardt, Ch. 7, p.158).

**Key historical moments:**
- King Ram Khamhaeng of Sukhothai (d. 1298) was a fervent Buddhist king who bore a Hindu name: Rama (Rush, p.40). His famous inscription at Sukhothai's mandala capital honored not only Buddha but also the local spirit of Phra Khaphung, "superior to all the spirits of the country" (Rush, p.39).
- King Tilokaraja of Chiang Mai (1455) built a temple in imitation of the Maha Bodhi temple at Bodh Gaya, India, and hosted a major Buddhist editorial assembly at the Seven Spires Monastery (Blackburn, p.151, 157).
- The Chakri dynasty (from 1782), each of whose kings has reigned as Rama (Rush, p.40).

**Distinctive local practices:**
- **Temple sleeping (Shan):** Old people commit to staying at the temple on *wan sin* (precept days), keeping eight precepts, meditating, and progressively withdrawing from worldly life (Eberhardt, Ch. 7, p.157-158).
- **Meditation with mak nap:** Buddhist "rosary" beads used with popped rice as markers. Practitioners repeat "anicca, dukkha, anatta" in prescribed rounds (Eberhardt, Ch. 7, p.158-159).
- **Paying Respect ceremony (kaentau):** Biannual village-wide ritual where young people visit temple sleepers' homes with flower bouquets, popped rice, and gift bundles. Old people chant blessings while visitors sit in *waai* (respectful hand position). Occurs at New Year in the 5th month and at end of rainy season in the 11th month (Eberhardt, Ch. 7, p.159-160).
- **First-month bathing ceremony for newborns:** Temple sleepers tie white strings around wrists of newborns while uttering blessings. Cannot be performed without temple sleepers present (Eberhardt, Ch. 7, p.161).
- **Wedding blessings:** Temple sleepers tie white strings around wrists of bride and groom. Essential ritual officiant role (Eberhardt, Ch. 7, p.161).
- Spirit beliefs persist alongside Buddhism: Thai *phi* (spirits) and Burmese *nats* were folded into Theravada practice (Rush, p.44). King Ram Khamhaeng honored the local spirit of Phra Khaphung alongside Buddha (Rush, p.39).

### Myanmar (Burma)

**Political context and how it shapes Buddhist life:**
- Once a proud kingdom like Thailand, Burma was defeated by British armies in the 19th century and subsumed within the British Empire (Rush, p.8).
- Independence in 1948 brought a dysfunctional democracy, then Ne Win's military rule wedding Buddhism and socialism (Rush, p.8).
- By the 1980s, Burma was "backward, isolated, and riven by armed rebellions" (Rush, p.8).
- Aung San Suu Kyi's democracy movement and subsequent reforms ended isolation, but "the house of Myanmar remains bitterly divided" with hundreds of thousands of minority subjects fleeing (Rush, p.8).
- The military government (pre-2011) sought legitimacy through "strategic promotion of Buddhism" (Edwards, p.175).
- The intensification of Buddhist nationalism rendered Muslims "especially precarious," culminating in the Rohingya crisis and ethnic cleansing (Edwards, p.175).
- The State Sangha Maha Nayaka Committee is the government-appointed council of senior Buddhist monks. It issued a statement against Christians "appropriating" (*yu ngin*) Theravada Buddhist terms, warning against any further appropriation by "people of other religions" (*a'cha batha win*) (Edwards, p.177).
- The committee also attempted to ban Ma Ba Tha, the main Buddhist nationalist group spreading hate speech against Muslims (Edwards, p.177).
- Myanmar had one of the world's strictest censorship regimes. The Press Scrutiny Board (est. 1962) required all printed materials to be submitted prior to publication. Foreign radio stations were jammed (Edwards, p.178).
- Christians were given a list of approximately 100 "Buddhist" words (including *metta*, *dukkha*, and *paya*) they were told not to use in religious publications (Edwards, p.178).

**Buddhist institutional structure:**
- Theravada Buddhism is the dominant religion, deeply embedded in Burmese language (vocabulary draws heavily on Pali) (Edwards, p.178).
- The Sangha Maha Nayaka Committee regulates religious terminology and practice (Edwards, p.177).
- Sitagu Sayadaw is described as "one of Myanmar's most famous monks" - his poster appears alongside Buddha images and Shwedagon Pagoda photographs in ordinary homes (Edwards, p.180).
- Buddhism in Myanmar is intertwined with national belonging: the question "Who belongs in the new Myanmar?" is partly answered through Buddhist identity (Edwards, p.175).

**Key historical moments:**
- Arrival of Adoniram Judson, American Baptist missionary, in Rangoon in 1813. He brought the first printing press to Burma and translated the Bible into Burmese (completed 1834), still used today (Edwards, p.178).
- Judson wrote: "It is more difficult for a Burman to become a Christian than it is to extract a tooth from a tiger's mouth" (Edwards, p.178).
- February 1, 2021: Myanmar's military seized power in a coup (Edwards, p.183, note 8).
- The 2015 election and NLD victory / Aung San Suu Kyi's rise to power, ending five decades of military rule (Edwards, p.175, 180).
- The "Love Joy Peace Festival" organized by Billy Graham Evangelistic Association - set to be the largest Christian public event since the military expelled foreign missionaries in 1962 (Edwards, p.179).

**Distinctive local practices:**
- **Paya** is a Burmanized Pali word used by Christians to refer to "God" and by Buddhists to refer to the Buddha. The dual usage creates anxiety: Buddhists worry about confusion of God with Buddha, and about the Christian term "Everlasting God" (*tawara paya*) contrasting with the Buddha's teaching that all things are impermanent (Edwards, p.178).
- **Nalehmu** - a Burmese political concept meaning "mutual understanding," referring to personalized networks of reciprocity and trust that helped ordinary people survive military rule (Edwards, p.182).
- Buddhist-Christian encounters involve complex "translation" dynamics: Christians oscillate between "domesticating" the gospel (using Buddhist concepts like karma, merit, nirvana) and "foreignizing" it (keeping it distinct) (Edwards, p.176-181).
- Door-to-door evangelism in Buddhist neighborhoods, where evangelists like Suan draw on Buddhist vocabulary (*dukkha*, *kutho*, *neikban*, *kaun'hmu kutho*) to communicate Christian concepts (Edwards, p.180).

### Cambodia

**Political context:**
- Site of "one of Southeast Asia's monstrous modern atrocities" - the Khmer Rouge "Killing Fields" (1975-1978) still hang over the nation (Rush, p.8).
- Heirs of the once-monumental kingdom of Angkor (800s-1400s). 19th-century kings steered their diminished kingdom to safety under French protection (Rush, p.8).
- Norodom Sihanouk was repackaged as president after France departed in 1953, attempted to steer through Cold War, but US bombing led to Khmer Rouge triumph and murder of over two million people (Rush, p.9).
- After 1979: long recuperation involving occupation by Vietnam and the UN. Survives today as a small Buddhist kingdom with a constitutional monarch (Sihanouk's son) and a quasi-elected strongman prime minister who is a former Khmer Rouge commander (Rush, p.9).
- National leaders invoke democracy and representative government, but democracy is "highly compromised by authoritarian power structures" (Rush, p.9).

**Buddhist institutional structure:**
- The great Khmer kings of Angkor were both Hindu and Buddhist. The temple complex of Angkor contains both Angkor Wat (honoring Siva and Vishnu) and the Bayon (Bodhisattva Avalokitesvara, King Jayavarman VII) (Rush, p.37).
- Buddhism in Cambodia was nearly destroyed by the Khmer Rouge but has been rebuilt as a pillar of national identity.

**Key historical moments:**
- Angkor's dominance over the Tonle Sap and lower Mekong basin (800s-1400s) (Rush, p.28).
- The Khmer Rouge genocide (1975-1978) and its aftermath.
- Angkor Wat as monumental mandala center (Rush, p.38) - wars rendered in stone on its walls, involving armies of foot soldiers and war elephants (Rush, p.34).

### Vietnam

**Political context:**
- Vietnam's independence movement under Ho Chi Minh triumphed over both France and the United States - "a legendary epic in modern Southeast Asian history" (Rush, p.10).
- "The once domineering Confucian kingdom," humiliated and colonized by France in the 19th century, abandoned feudal trappings to rise as Southeast Asia's strongest Communist state (Rush, p.10).
- Today with 95+ million people, the Communist Party rules, but market forces are dominant and "old enemies are becoming new allies" (Rush, p.10).

**Buddhist institutional structure:**
- Vietnam is the exception to the Indianization pattern: it emerged under Chinese civilization's sway. The Vietnamese homeland along the Red River was a colony of China for nearly a thousand years (Han, Sui, Tang dynasties) (Rush, p.44).
- Three religions of Vietnam: Mahayana Buddhism, Daoism, and Confucianism (Rush, p.45).
- Confucian values of social hierarchy (children defer to parents, wives to husbands) underpinned loyalty to the Vietnamese emperor, who ruled Chinese-style as the Son of Heaven (Rush, p.45).
- Vietnamese Buddhist practice includes veneration of Bodhisattva Kuanyin and Daoist lore, mingled with local guardian spirits (Rush, p.45).
- Scholar officials modeled on China governed the realm; prospective officials were examined competitively on Chinese classics of statecraft, philosophy, and poetry (Rush, p.45).

**Distinctive local practices:**
- Vietnamese Buddhist practice blends Mahayana Buddhism with Confucian filial piety and Daoist elements - a triple-religion fusion unlike the Theravada mainland.
- Local guardian spirits held imperial appointments under the emperor (Rush, p.45).

### Laos

**Political context:**
- "A traditionally Buddhist society much like its neighbors," formed from princely domains amalgamated into a French protectorate in the 1890s (Rush, p.9).
- Drawn into Vietnam's war for independence via French Indochina connection. Years of confusion and turmoil ended in 1975 with Communist-led government (Rush, p.9).
- The Lao People's Democratic Republic remains "a highly agrarian and still largely Buddhist society where the largest city, Vientiane, hosts fewer than 1 million people" (Rush, p.9).
- Former royal capital Luang Prabang "is renowned for its grace" (Rush, p.9).
- The lowland Lao majority populates narrow river valleys, yet 90% of territory rises above 600 feet populated by non-Lao hill peoples (Rush, p.9-10).
- Globalization reaches deep into Laos from every side, especially from China (shares 263-mile border). Chinese companies are building "casinos, five-star hotels, banana and rubber plantations, and dozens of hydroelectric dams." China accounts for 40% of foreign investment in Laos (Rush, p.18).
- In Laos and Vietnam there have "always been competing national narratives - communist, anticommunist, and others - all laying claim to the nation's past" (Thongchai, p.4).

## B. BUDDHIST CONCEPTS & PRACTICES

### Textual Traditions and How They Shape Lived Religion

**Pali Canon as living practice (Blackburn):**
- Texts are not merely repositories of doctrine but are "central to vital human activities that take place within institutions and contexts of practice we now call religious" (Blackburn, p.151).
- Buddhist texts were understood to contain "powerful words and syllables, potent Buddha-speech with transformative properties." They could be deployed as chant or written talisman to access the "superhuman power of Sakyamuni Buddha" (Blackburn, p.157).
- Possessing religious texts, or supporting their production, "is often (especially in a manuscript culture) a display of wealth and power" (Blackburn, p.151).
- The textual corpus extends beyond canonical materials to include "scriptural commentary and compendia, as well as poetry, theological and philosophical treatises, expert witness on the privileged sciences (grammar, astrology and astronomy, literary theory, hermeneutics, medicine), and stories of saints, teachers, and heroes" (Blackburn, p.161).

**Textual communities (Blackburn):**
- "Those who participate in shared practices of reading, writing, listening, interpretation, and performance, with reference to the same body of texts...may be said to constitute a textual community" (Blackburn, p.164).
- In the medieval Buddhist world, Pali-language texts originating in the Indic world reached the SE Asian mainland through flows of monks and monastic lineages from Lanka (Sri Lanka). These monastic ordination lineages inaugurated shared forms of meditative and ritual practice, devotional style, and expectations of courtly culture (Blackburn, p.165).
- In the 19th century, Lankan and Burmese monks corresponded about how Pali Buddhist texts could rebut Christian missionary attacks (Blackburn, p.165).

**Temple sleepers as textual community (Eberhardt):**
- Old people who become temple sleepers undergo "something of a crash course in what amounts to a Buddhist 'Great Books Program'" - hearing sermons, scriptural readings, and exegesis (Eberhardt, Ch. 7, p.162).
- They become repositories of tradition and authority, deferred to in "matters of ritual knowledge and proper behavior" (Eberhardt, Ch. 7, p.162).
- Community members reinforce this by claiming "only the old people" understand the readings (Eberhardt, Ch. 7, p.162).

**Textual efficacy and royal patronage (Blackburn):**
- King Tilokaraja of Chiang Mai understood that protecting Buddhist texts was an act of devotional generosity that improved his karmic merit and prospects for rebirth (Blackburn, p.158).
- The editorial assembly and textual recitation at the Seven Spires Monastery were "a means through which the king could accumulate marks of status and authority" - partly political maneuvers within monastic rivalries and expansionary politics (Blackburn, p.159).
- Tilokaraja cultivated an "Asokan persona" - modeling himself on King Asoka (3rd century BCE) who purified Buddhist teachings and sponsored an editorial assembly (Blackburn, p.159).

**Language choice as power (Blackburn):**
- Pali was a language of "deep social resonance" like Sanskrit, Arabic, Persian, Latin, and classical Chinese. Using it in recitation or composition "asserts high status and direct access to the most highly privileged textual materials" (Blackburn, p.162).
- The monk Valivita Saranankara in 18th-century Lanka composed a bilingual commentary (*Sararthadipani* / *Illuminator of Essential Meaning*) that positioned him as heir to desirable monastic traditions (Blackburn, p.162-163).

### Syncretism: How Buddhism Blends with Animism, Hinduism, Islam, Confucianism

**Animism as substrate (Rush):**
- "Belief in such spirits - the anima or life in all things - forms the original, primal form of spirituality in Southeast Asia" (Rush, p.38).
- Even as Southeast Asians adapted Hindu, Buddhist, Muslim, and Christian beliefs, "they never wholly abandoned the deep belief that, for example, rice is alive and that the songs of birds, the sudden chills of a breeze, and the stirring of volcanoes bear messages" (Rush, p.38-39).
- Thai *phi* (spirits) and Burmese *nats* were folded into Theravada practice (Rush, p.44).

**Hindu-Buddhist fusion (Rush):**
- "Southeast Asians partook of Hinduism and Buddhism more or less simultaneously," hence the long period is called "Hindu-Buddhist era" (Rush, p.37).
- In Indianized states, kings became *devaraja* (god-kings) representing themselves as incarnations of Hindu deities (Siva, Vishnu) or Buddhist monarchs in the manner of Asoka (Rush, p.37).
- The Jataka tales retelling Buddha's past-life reincarnations were carved on Borobudur (c. 800) and recounted throughout Burma, Thailand, Cambodia, and Laos (Rush, p.39).
- The Ramayana and Mahabharata became Southeast Asian stories, forming the basis for drama, dance, shadow-puppet theater (Javanese *wayang*), and popular culture (Rush, p.39).

**Islam and older traditions (Rush/Sevea):**
- Islam in Java formed "a particularly complex fusion" clinging to Hindu-Buddhist-animist elements in "a sort of mystic synthesis" (Rush, p.43).
- Southeast Asian Muslims "continued to make offerings to venerated ancient spirits, and they remained wary of, and in awe of, certain spirit-laden sites" (Rush, p.43).

**Shan spirit beliefs alongside Buddhism (Eberhardt):**
- *Khwan* (soul-stuff) transforms to *phi* (spirit-being) at death. The old person's ascetic withdrawal helps neutralize the life force for a "swift and trouble-free transformation" (Eberhardt, Ch. 7, p.163).
- Death of a younger person IS frightening because their khwan may not have severed ties with the living (Eberhardt, Ch. 7, p.163-164).
- People are overtly concerned with both immediate fate after death (going to *moeng phi*, Buddhist heavens) and eventual rebirth in *moeng khon* (the realm of humans), hoping for an auspicious rebirth (Eberhardt, Ch. 7, p.163).

**Buddhist-Christian translation in Myanmar (Edwards):**
- The evangelical tract "The Drowning Man" (*ye niq thu*) contrasts merit (*kutho*) with grace (*che zu daw*) - "Jesus saves you" vs. "the Buddha only shows you how to save yourself" (Edwards, p.176).
- Evangelists draw equivalences between Buddhist and Christian concepts: *dukkha* = suffering; *neikban* = nirvana; *kaun kin* = heaven; *tawara a'theq* = eternal life; *kaun'hmu kutho* = merit. But these "points of contact" also open possibilities for misunderstanding (Edwards, p.176-181).
- The elderly Buddhist woman Daw Than Than's response to evangelism: "Well, yes, all [religions] are the same (*tu tu*)" - the opposite of what evangelists hoped for (Edwards, p.180).

### Regional Identity and Religion

**"Southeast Asia" as concept (Rush):**
- Southeast Asia is "a sprawling neighborhood of hot countries that straddles the equator. Its eleven nations lie between India and China" (Rush, p.1).
- Contains 625 million people, around 9% of world population, stretching 3,000 miles (Rush, p.1).
- "Here societies drawing from Buddhism, Hinduism, Islam, Christianity, and Confucianism (alongside myriad other traditions) have rubbed shoulders for centuries" (Rush, p.1).
- The region bifurcated: "a Buddhist mainland and a Muslim archipelago with a Buddhist-Muslim cusp in the mid-to-lower Malay Peninsula" (Rush, p.44).

**Nation as contested identity (Thongchai):**
- National histories in Southeast Asia are "a 'true' or 'correct' collective ledger, computed in accordance with a scale of spiritual merit and demerit, as well as a repository of the 'national spirit and ideology'" (Thongchai, p.5).
- Thongchai argues for "history at the interstices" - exploring margins where national narratives break down, including the Karen, Mon, Kachin, Aceh peoples who never became nations or resist integration (Thongchai, p.12).
- Ethnic and religious minorities are positioned at the margins through "the construction of citizenship, of discrimination, exclusion, and suppression" (Thongchai, p.12).

**Localization as key process (Thongchai):**
- Every wave of globalization (Indianization, Islam, colonialism, modernity) undergoes localization. "Though the Indic influence in local cultures was widespread, they never made Southeast Asia become Indian. Nor did colonial hegemony or Westernization make us Euro-American" (Thongchai, p.13).
- The concept of *sakdina* (Thai term for feudal/status hierarchy) was transformed by radical Thai Marxists in the 1950s from an "exalted, honorable connotation into a derogatory one" - an example of localization of global Marxism (Thongchai, p.14).

### Sacred Sites, Relics, Pilgrimage

**Angkor Wat** - monumental temple complex marking the center of the Khmer mandala. Contains both Hindu temple (honoring Siva and Vishnu) and the Buddhist Bayon complex (Rush, p.37-38).

**Borobudur** - stupendous Buddhist stupa in Java, legacy of the 8th-century Sailendra kingdom. Its architectural plan features "tiers of concentric square and round terraces (adorned with exquisite Buddha images) rise like a pyramid to a single bell-shaped stupa at the top" (Rush, p.37). Jataka tales carved on its walls c. 800 CE.

**Pagan** - temples and stupas in present-day Myanmar, another monumental mandala center (Rush, p.38).

**Seven Spires Monastery (Chiang Mai)** - site of Tilokaraja's Buddhist editorial assembly where canonical texts were purified and recited. Included a *bodhi* tree transferred from one of Chiang Mai's most powerful existing Buddhist sites (Blackburn, p.151).

**Maha Bodhi temple (Bodh Gaya, India)** - the site of Sakyamuni Buddha's enlightenment, which Tilokaraja's Chiang Mai temple was built to imitate (Blackburn, p.151).

**Shwedagon Pagoda** - Myanmar's most famous Buddhist site; its image appears in ordinary homes alongside Buddha statues (Edwards, p.180).

**Luang Prabang** - former royal capital of Laos, "renowned for its grace" (Rush, p.9).

**Keramat shrines** - Islamic miracle-working shrines in Malay-Indonesian world. The shrine of Sharifa Siti Maryam al-'Aydarus (d. 1853) in Singapore's Kallang was a major pilgrimage site until demolished for urban redevelopment in 2010 (Sevea, p.3-5).

### Religious Authority and How It's Negotiated

**Temple sleepers as authority figures (Eberhardt):**
- Temple sleepers gain special powers through precept-keeping: "the power to generate merit for those who approach them and ask their blessings, but also more general powers such as the power to heal, to drive out spirits, and to sanctify something with their blessings" (Eberhardt, Ch. 7, p.160).
- "The longer they have been in the role of temple sleeper, the more powerful they are thought to be" (Eberhardt, Ch. 7, p.160).
- Old people are held to a "higher standard of morality than ordinary Buddhists" - if a temple sleeper drinks alcohol, loses temper, or kills a chicken, "it is scandalous in a way that it would not be if a younger person committed these same acts" (Eberhardt, Ch. 7, p.159).

**Royal Buddhist patronage as authority (Blackburn):**
- Tilokaraja competed with rival Trailokaraja at Ayutthaya for regional primacy. Both acted in "a distinctively Buddhist imperial idiom, aspiring to recognition as righteous rulers on a cosmic scale" through military might AND through acts informed by Buddhist narrative models (Blackburn, p.159-160).
- Sponsoring editorial assemblies and reciting texts generated social capital and marks of status (Blackburn, p.159).

**Mandala politics (Rush):**
- Power in mandala kingdoms was not territorial but radiating: "the king's power radiated out from the capital and...passed by degree through each successive concentric circle, attenuated and eventually died out altogether" (Rush, p.29).
- Kings were *devaraja* (god-kings), but succession was contested: "Kings had many sons...queens and their progeny formed factions at court...succession disputes were routine and often violent" (Rush, p.32).

**State regulation of religious language (Edwards):**
- Myanmar's Sangha Maha Nayaka Committee fixed a boundary around "Buddhist language," preventing Christians from using Pali-derived terms like *thanga* (Sangha), *metta*, *dukkha*, *paya* (Edwards, p.177-178).
- This was simultaneously about protecting Buddhism AND regulating minority religious expression.

## C. CASE STUDIES & REAL STORIES

### 1. Uncle Toen the Great Hunter (Eberhardt, Ch. 7, p.156)
**Setting:** Baan Kaung Mu, October 27, 1981. Uncle Toen visits the ethnographer's house with whiskey and pheasant traps. He recounts omens that suggest he should quit hunting and become a temple sleeper: missing an animal at close range (unprecedented for him), catching nothing while fishing.
**Key quote:** "If you haven't known sorrow and evil, you can't really know happiness and virtue. I've spent my entire life hunting and have been fairly successful at it. Lately, though, I've been getting omens that make me think I should quit hunting and start sleeping at the temple."
**Outcome:** Uncle Toen was dead within ten years, having never fully made the transition.

### 2. Boys Anticipating Novice Ordination (Eberhardt, Ch. 8, p.171-172)
**Setting:** Baan Kaung Mu, March 23, 1981. Ten-year-old Tsit and his friend Weng discuss becoming novice monks.
**Tsit's reaction:** Ambivalent - worried about being lonely and sleeping away from home, unable to eat after noon. When asked if it will be fun: "N- er, yes, it will be."
**Weng's reaction:** Enthusiastic, can hardly contain excitement, chatters happily about it.
**Key insight:** Both boys "have widely divergent feelings" yet share "the culturally nurtured conviction that it was inevitable." The act of anticipation itself shapes identity formation.

### 3. Children's Understanding of Merit (Eberhardt, Ch. 7, p.161-162)
Age-specific progression of understanding *kuso* (merit):
- **4-year-old girl:** Merit is "when people raise their hands in a *waai*. Old people have a lot [of merit]."
- **7-year-old boy:** "It's at the temple. You sleep at the temple."
- **8-year-old girl:** "It's words, Pali words." Merit comes from becoming a nun and chanting scriptures.
- **11-year-old girl:** Merit comes from making offerings and having a boy ordained as a novice. Good merit means "you don't get sick...you have a lot of money, you're wealthy and prosperous."
- **13-year-old girl:** Good merit means "you will be reborn as a good person. When you're reborn in another life, you won't be a bad person."

### 4. Christian Evangelism to Daw Than Than (Edwards, p.180-181)
**Setting:** Thanlyin, Myanmar, November 7, 2015 (day before historic election). Evangelists Suan and Kim visit elderly Buddhist woman Daw Than Than in her one-room thatch hut. Above her was an altar with a Buddha image, Shwedagon Pagoda photograph, and poster of Sitagu Sayadaw.
**The encounter:** Suan drew together Christian and Buddhist concepts (grace, merit, suffering, nirvana, heaven) in a single soteriological calculation. Kim intervened to emphasize difference: "Your karma is still tangling you (*wit ma kyut*), right? You might become a hungry ghost (*pate tar*)."
**Daw Than Than's response:** "Well, yes, all [religions] are the same (*tu tu*)." She declined to accept Jesus as personal savior.
**Key tension:** Too much domestication risks making Christianity seem "the same"; too much foreignization risks making it incomprehensible.

### 5. The Drowning Man Tract on the Irrawaddy (Edwards, p.175-176)
**Setting:** A boat traveling down the Irrawaddy River, Myanmar. A Christian evangelist began distributing copies of "The Drowning Man" gospel tract during a violent storm.
**Outcome:** Buddhists received the pamphlet (featuring a drawing of a person flailing in water) as a "bad omen" (*ni mak ma kaun bu*) that they might be about to drown. Instead of opening a door to Jesus, the misstep slammed it shut.

### 6. Sharifa Siti Maryam's Shrine Demolition (Sevea, p.3-5)
**Setting:** Kallang, Singapore, April 2010. Government ordered exhumation and removal of the keramat's burial ground for urban redevelopment.
**Events:** Laborers employed to remove the shrine were "bleeding, falling and struggling" and unable to work until caretaker Wak 'Ali prayed for forgiveness. Despite digging extensively, workers were unable to find the saint's bones. The caretaker declared: "She's still here!"
**Key significance:** Shows how sacred sites resist secular authority; miracle stories circulated throughout Southeast Asian shrine communities.

### 7. Tilokaraja's Buddhist Editorial Assembly (Blackburn, p.157-159)
**Setting:** Chiang Mai, 1455. King Tilokaraja, struggling politically against rival factions and the growing power of Ayutthaya to the south, undertook a massive act of Buddhist patronage.
**Actions:** Built a temple imitating Bodh Gaya, installed a bodhi tree, constructed spaces for veneration, scriptural recitation, and a monastic library. Hosted a gathering of Buddhist monks to purify and recite the canon.
**Significance:** A simultaneously devotional and political act - protecting Buddhist texts was an act of karmic merit-making while the editorial assembly generated social capital and legitimized his contested rule.

## D. ENCOUNTER SEEDS (Game Events)

### 1. The Hunter's Omens
**Situation:** You are an aging hunter/fisher in a Shan village. Lately you have been experiencing strange omens: missed shots at close range, empty fishing nets, a fish that trapped your hand. Village elders say these are signs you should give up hunting and become a temple sleeper.
**Choices:**
- A) Heed the omens and begin the transition to temple sleeping - give up hunting, take the eight precepts
- B) Acknowledge the omens but delay - "there are still things I need to teach my apprentice"
- C) Dismiss the omens as coincidence and continue hunting
**Stakes:** Karmic progression vs. worldly attachment; community respect vs. personal identity; spiritual development timing

### 2. The Novice Ordination Decision
**Situation:** You are a 10-year-old Shan boy. Your village is holding a novice ordination festival. Your parents expect you to ordain. Your friend is excited, but you are worried about sleeping away from home and not eating after noon.
**Choices:**
- A) Embrace the ordination enthusiastically
- B) Go through with it reluctantly, focusing on the festival aspects
- C) Try to delay or avoid it (creating family tension)
**Stakes:** Family honor, community expectations, personal fear vs. cultural duty; beginning of Buddhist identity formation

### 3. The Temple Sleeper's Scandal
**Situation:** You are an elderly temple sleeper known for your blessings and healing powers. One evening, you lose your temper at a grandchild and are seen by neighbors. A village gossip spreads the word.
**Choices:**
- A) Acknowledge the lapse publicly and recommit to the precepts with visible austerity
- B) Ignore the gossip and hope it passes
- C) Use your spiritual authority to redirect the narrative
**Stakes:** Temple sleepers are held to higher moral standards than ordinary people. Loss of respect means loss of perceived spiritual power and community role.

### 4. The Paying Respect Ceremony
**Situation:** It is the biannual *kaentau* ceremony. As a young person, you must decide which temple sleepers to visit. There are fifty in your village, and you cannot visit them all.
**Choices:**
- A) Visit only close relatives and neighbors (practical but potentially disrespectful to others)
- B) Try to visit as many as possible, rushing through each encounter
- C) Focus on a few key elders known for powerful blessings, bringing extra-generous gifts
**Stakes:** Social relationships, community standing, accumulated merit through respectful gestures

### 5. The Drowning Man Tract
**Situation:** You are a Buddhist passenger on a boat traveling down the Irrawaddy River. A violent storm breaks out. A Christian stranger begins handing out pamphlets showing a drowning person. The pamphlet is called "The Drowning Man."
**Choices:**
- A) Take the pamphlet as a terrible omen, throw it away, and demand the person stop
- B) Politely accept it but feel disturbed - is this a warning about the storm?
- C) Read it curiously - what does this foreign religion offer?
**Stakes:** Superstition vs. curiosity; interfaith encounter; the meaning of omens during danger

### 6. The Evangelist at Your Door (Myanmar)
**Situation:** A young Christian woman knocks on your door in peri-urban Yangon. She begins speaking about suffering (*dukkha*), how merit (*kutho*) is never enough, and how grace (*che zu daw*) from Jesus can deliver you to "everlasting heaven" (*tawara kaun kin boun*). She uses Buddhist vocabulary but her message is clearly Christian.
**Choices:**
- A) "All religions are the same (*tu tu*)" - politely agree and disengage
- B) Engage seriously with the theological differences between merit and grace
- C) Become offended that she is appropriating Buddhist terms and tell her to stop
- D) Invite her to visit the monastery with you instead
**Stakes:** Buddhist identity, openness to other traditions, the politics of religious language in Myanmar

### 7. The Sacred Shrine vs. Urban Development
**Situation:** The government announces plans to demolish a beloved saint's shrine in your neighborhood to build a new development. The shrine has stood for over 150 years and is a pilgrimage site. Workers are already arriving with bulldozers.
**Choices:**
- A) Join the devotees who gather to protect the shrine, telling miracle stories about what happened to others who tried to destroy it
- B) Accept the government's decision but try to negotiate relocation of sacred relics
- C) See the development as progress and modernity
**Stakes:** Sacred heritage vs. modernization; community identity; who controls sacred space

### 8. The Royal Textual Assembly
**Situation:** You are a minor lord in 15th-century Chiang Mai. The king has called for a grand Buddhist editorial assembly at his new monastery. Attending means pledging loyalty and resources. But the rival king of Ayutthaya has also invited monks from your region to his own assembly.
**Choices:**
- A) Attend Tilokaraja's assembly at Chiang Mai - local loyalty
- B) Accept Trailokaraja of Ayutthaya's invitation - potentially more powerful patron
- C) Send representatives to both while remaining uncommitted
**Stakes:** Political allegiance through religious patronage; which Buddhist textual lineage to support; the intersection of spiritual merit and political power

### 9. The Aging Decision
**Situation:** You are in your mid-forties. Your physical labor is slowing. Your children are growing up. People in the village have started calling you "old" and asking when you will start sleeping at the temple. But your house is not yet built, your last child has not finished school, and you need cash for upcoming expenses.
**Choices:**
- A) Begin the transition now - start keeping precepts on wan sin, gradually withdraw from farm labor
- B) Delay until practical matters are resolved - "after the house is built, after my last child finishes school"
- C) Resist the label entirely - you feel too young for "old age"
**Stakes:** The tension between material obligations and spiritual progression; the community's developmental timeline vs. personal readiness; karmic investment in this life vs. next

### 10. The Buddhist Word Police
**Situation:** You are a Christian pastor in Myanmar. The Sangha Maha Nayaka Committee has issued a statement banning non-Buddhists from using approximately 100 Pali-derived words in their publications, including *metta* (loving-kindness), *dukkha* (suffering), and *paya* (a word you use for God, but which Buddhists use for the Buddha).
**Choices:**
- A) Comply fully and develop entirely new Christian vocabulary, risking incomprehensibility
- B) Continue using the terms discreetly, risking legal consequences
- C) Adopt a middle path - "domesticate" some terms while "foreignizing" others
- D) Challenge the ruling publicly as censorship
**Stakes:** Minority religious survival, translation as power, the boundary between religions in a Buddhist-majority state

## E. KEY TERMINOLOGY

### Pali Terms with Translations
- **anicca** - impermanence (one of the three marks of existence)
- **dukkha** - suffering (one of the three marks of existence)
- **anatta** - not-self (one of the three marks of existence)
- **nikpan** / **neikban** - nirvana (Shan/Burmese forms of Pali *nibbana*)
- **kuso** / **kutho** - merit (Shan/Burmese forms of Pali *kusala*)
- **kam** - karma (Shan form; the general stamp of a person's life circumstances)
- **wot** - karmic retribution (Shan; explains specific events as residue of past immoral acts)
- **sutta** - discourse/teaching of the Buddha
- **dharma** / **dhamma** - the teachings; cosmic law; Buddhist doctrine
- **metta** - loving-kindness
- **paya** - Burmanized Pali word for "the Buddha"; also used by Christians to refer to God, creating controversy
- **tawara paya** - "Everlasting God" (Christian Burmese term, contentious)
- **sangha** / **thanga** - the community of monks (Burmese version: *thanga*)
- **cakkavattin** - universal monarch / wheel-turning king (ideal Buddhist ruler)
- **devaraja** - god-king (Sanskrit-Pali concept adopted by SE Asian rulers)
- **bodhi** - enlightenment; the bodhi tree marks Buddha's awakening

### Country-Specific Local Terms

**Shan (Northern Thailand):**
- **wan sin** - precept day (lit. "morality day"); days when temple sleepers keep eight precepts
- **khon thao** - "old people" (term for temple sleepers / elders)
- **khon naun tsaung** - "people who sleep at the temple" (temple sleepers)
- **mak nap** - Buddhist "rosary" / meditation beads
- **phaan** - large lacquered container taken to the temple when sleeping there
- **waai** - respectful hand gesture (hands pressed together, raised to forehead)
- **kaentau** - Paying Respect ceremony (biannual village-wide ritual honoring temple sleepers)
- **kai toen** - pheasant traps
- **khwan** - soul-stuff / life force (pre-Buddhist Shan concept)
- **phi** - spirit-being (what khwan transforms into at death)
- **moeng phi** - Buddhist heavens (where one goes immediately after death)
- **moeng khon** - realm of humans (where one hopes to be reborn auspiciously)
- **hai** - ignorant (descriptor for children before development)
- **kuso** - merit (Shan form)

**Burmese (Myanmar):**
- **paya** - term for the Buddha; controversially also used by Christians for God
- **neikban** - nirvana (Burmese form)
- **kaun kin** - heaven (Burmese)
- **tawara kaun kin boun** - "everlasting heaven" (Christian Burmese term)
- **tawara a'theq** - eternal life (Christian Burmese term)
- **kaun'hmu kutho** - merit from good deeds (Burmese)
- **che zu daw** - grace (Christian Burmese term)
- **nauk bawa** - "next life" (Burmese)

- **ye niq thu** - "The Drowning Man" (title of Christian tract)
- **ni mak ma kaun bu** - "bad omen" (Burmese)
- **na leh nain** - "outsiders can understand" (Burmese)

- **a'cha batha win** - "people of other religions" (official Burmese designation)

**Malay-Indonesian:**
- **keramat** / **karamat** - miracle-working individual AND miracle-working shrine at their burial site (from Arabic *karamat*)

## CROSS-CUTTING THEMES FOR GAME DESIGN

### 1. The Life-Cycle as Buddhist Path
The Shan model (Eberhardt) provides a complete life-cycle framework for the game:
- **Childhood:** Learning about merit through observation; associating merit with old people and temples
- **Youth:** Novice ordination as culturally expected transformation; ambivalence about leaving home
- **Adulthood:** Material pursuits (farming, trade, family); keeping five precepts; sponsoring ordinations and merit-making ceremonies
- **Middle age:** Gradually reducing physical labor; beginning to identify as "old"; experiencing omens
- **Old age:** Becoming a temple sleeper; keeping eight precepts; meditation; gaining spiritual powers; preparing for death
- **Death:** Transformation from *khwan* to *phi*; the quality of one's final thoughts determines rebirth

### 2. Merit as Currency
Merit (*kuso*/*kutho*) functions as the game's spiritual economy:
- Earned through: temple sleeping, keeping precepts, making offerings, sponsoring ordinations, feeding monks, reciting scripture
- Spent/invested toward: better rebirth, community respect, spiritual powers, protection from spirits
- Children learn it progressively; adults accumulate it; old people are its embodiment

### 3. The Mandala Political System
Power radiates from centers and attenuates with distance. The player exists within concentric circles of political/religious authority. Loyalty is negotiated, not fixed. This maps directly onto game-world political structures.

### 4. Localization and Syncretism
Every foreign influence (Indian, Chinese, Islamic, Christian, modern) gets localized. The game can model this through encounter events where the player must navigate between foreign ideas and local practice.

### 5. Religious Encounter as Translation Problem
Edwards' Myanmar material shows that cross-religious encounters are fundamentally about translation - finding "points of contact" while maintaining meaningful difference. This creates rich game scenarios where word choice matters.
